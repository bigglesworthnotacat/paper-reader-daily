## Abstract 

AI-assisted coding agents are bottlenecked by input-token cost. Two pathologies of raw hu-man input drive most of the overhead: tok-enization overhead (2–3× more tokens for non-English text) and structural entropy in conversa-tional prompts (greedy retrieval and verbose de-coding). Existing methods act reactively, com-pressing already-bloated contexts or cascading on failure. We introduce a pre-flight, edge-side prompt-rewriting middleware between the developer and the cloud agent. A local Llama 3.2 

(3B) via Ollama performs three operations in a sub-second pass: cross-lingual translation into the cheaper English token space, structural rewriting into a compact [CONTEXT] /[TASK] 

format, and a regex-validated rewrite-with-fallback guarded by a 5% token-budget thresh-old so the optimized payload is never larger than the original. We evaluate on OMH-Polyglot , the polyglot-specification arm of our OMH (Ock-

Multilingual-Hard) benchmark family ( 200 

instances translated into Turkish, Arabic, Chinese, or code-switched mixtures; per-prompt tokenization-overhead ratio 2.05 ×

on average, max 6.15 ×). Across three commercial backends with three runs per condition, the middleware reduces prompt tokens by 34 –47% and total tokens by 8.3% ,

8.3% , and 18 .8% on gpt-3.5-turbo , gpt-4o ,and gemini-2.5-flash-lite . Accuracy is 99 .5% , 99 .5% (+1 .17 pp), and 98 .0% 

(+3 .00 pp). A names_only ablation isolates the function-name oracle: alone it decreases accuracy by 18 –20 pp on the two weaker backends and is neutral on gpt-4o , attributing the Gemini gain to the rewriter. Against LLMLingua-2 (Pan et al., 2024) at matched rate, our middleware strictly Pareto-dominates on OckScore across all three backends. Dollar-cost outcomes are asymmetric: −12 .4% 

on Gemini, −0.4% on gpt-4o , and +15 .1% 

on gpt-3.5-turbo .

## 1 Introduction 

AI-assisted IDEs such as Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and Claude Code now operate over repositories that exceed the model’s context window, so each chat turn inflates the prompt with retrieved code chunks, prior turns, and unstructured user instructions. At deployment scale, per-query overhead compounds into substantial financial and environmental cost (Du et al., 2026; Peng et al., 2025). A large fraction of this cost is paid before the cloud model runs, and originates from two com-pounding pathologies of the raw input. (i) To-kenization overhead : subword tokenizers used by frontier LLMs (e.g., cl100k_base ) are statis-tically optimized for English; equivalent content in Turkish, Chinese, or Arabic can require 2–3×

more tokens (Petrov et al., 2023; Teklehaymanot and Petrov, 2025). (ii) Structural entropy : devel-oper prompts are conversational and vague, driv-ing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) into greedy retrieval (Jimenez et al., 2024) and inducing verbose decoding (Ahuja et al., 2025). Existing remedies act reactively. Post-hoc to-ken compressors (Jiang et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2025) prune an already-bloated context, paying perplexity-computation overhead inside the interac-tive loop. Cost-aware prompt-search frameworks (Taherkhani et al., 2025; Zehle et al., 2025) are of-fline and require expensive cloud-side iterations on the API they aim to cheapen. Local–cloud cascad-ing (Lu et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2024) falls back to the large model with the original, unoptimized prompt the moment the small model fails. None

The pipeline runs on local hard-ware and incurs zero recursive cloud-API cost.

## 2 Related Work 

Prompt optimization and compression. APE (Zhou et al., 2023) and APO (Pryzant et al., 2023) search for instructions to maximize accuracy but ig-nore cost. Token-level compressors prune unimpor-tant tokens: SelectiveContext (Li et al., 2023) via self-information, LLMLingua (Jiang et al., 2023) via a dynamic budget controller, and EHPC (Fei et al., 2025) via attention-head skimming. Agent-Diet (Xiao et al., 2026) summarizes expired tra-jectory history. These either depend on perplex-ity from a reference LM (recursive cost) or prune already-bloated context after the fact. 

Cost-aware search and cascading. EPiC (Taherkhani et al., 2025) and CAPO (Zehle et al., 2025) are offline prompt-search frameworks requiring repeated cloud-side iterations. Model cascading (Chen et al., 2024) and the local–cloud MCCom framework (Lu et al., 2026) forward the raw prompt to the large model on escalation. EfficientXLang (Ahuja et al., 2025) reports that translating reasoning into English yields up to 40% 

token reductions, but treats this as observation rather than an applied middleware. 

Repository-level context and benchmarks. 

Repository-level generation requires reasoning across files (Liu et al., 2024). RepoCoder (Zhang et al., 2023) iteratively retrieves based on interme-diate drafts; GraphCoder (Liu et al., 2024) uses control- and data-flow graphs. SWEzze (Mech-taev et al., 2026) reduces SWE-Bench token bud-gets by > 50% while improving issue resolution. Vague prompts trigger greedy RAG over-fetching and dependency hallucinations (Hai et al., 2026; Black Duck Software, 2026; MorphLLM, 2026). All act after retrieval. On the evaluation side, Hu-manEval, MBPP, and SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2024) score correctness but ignore cost; OckBench (Du et al., 2026) and COFFE (Peng et al., 2025) jointly measure accuracy and efficiency but remain post-hoc measurement tools. 3 Methodology 

The middleware is a pre-flight layer that intervenes before the RAG cycle in agentic IDEs. It shifts prompt-refinement compute to a local Small Lan-guage Model (SLM), neutralizing linguistic and structural overhead before cloud dispatch. 

3.1 Architecture 

The system is a TypeScript gateway that intercepts every IDE query and forwards a rewritten payload to the cloud backend. The local reasoning layer runs Llama 3.2 (3B) (AI, 2024) via an Ollama 

instance and is responsible for cross-lingual trans-lation, removal of conversational filler, and deter-ministic structural enforcement. The cloud execu-tion layer (e.g., gpt-4o ) receives only the rewritten Bi-Block.

3.3 Structural Rewriting: Bi-Block and Tri-Block 

We deploy two variants of a label-bracketed schema. The empirical results in this paper use the Bi-Block. 

Bi-Block (benchmark proxy). The proxy emits two labeled blocks per task. [CONTEXT] contains a single constant sentence (“ Python coding task; tests are authoritative. ”)—a generic preamble, not workspace-derived metadata. [TASK] contains an SLM-produced one- or two-sentence English in-struction, followed by the original assert lines copied verbatim. The SLM is prompted to translate non-English text, strip filler, and mention every re-quired function name verbatim; it is forbidden from emitting code or section labels. Function names are additionally extracted from the assert lines by a deterministic regex and supplied to the SLM as a hint. 

Tri-Block (IDE extension only). The IDE ex-tension targets a setting where constraints are not given as executable tests, and emits a third 

[CONSTRAINTS] block containing style guides, naming conventions, performance targets, or se-curity notes extracted from the raw query. Results in this paper bear only on the Bi-Block. 

Decoding. In both variants the SLM is decoded greedily ( temperature = 0 , top_p = 0 .9, predic-tion budget 200 tokens for the proxy and 400 for the IDE extension) with stop sequences preventing label or code leakage. 

3.4 Validation and Token-Budget Guard 

Each rewrite is checked by a regex validator (validateLight ) that triggers regeneration if (i) the output is empty or shorter than 10 charac-ters, (ii) a Python code block is emitted, (iii) a filled <solution>...</solution> envelope is produced, or (iv) required block markers are miss-ing (Tri-Block). Up to two repair attempts are allowed. A token-budget guard estimates cost ( 1 token per non-ASCII char, 0.25 per ASCII char) and for-wards the original prompt unchanged whenever the rewrite is not at least 5% smaller. If repair attempts repeatedly fail validation, the proxy logs the vio-lations and forwards the raw prompt. The guard ensures the proxy never inflates the cloud-billed payload. 

## 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 The OMH Benchmark Family 

We retain the OckBench-Coding (Du et al., 2026) harness: each row is a natural-language problem 

string followed by authoritative Python assert s, so functional grading and OckScore are unchanged. We replace the content of those rows. The default OckBench-Coding split sits near accuracy ceiling on commercial models and offers little headroom for token-efficiency mechanisms. 

OMH (Ock-Multilingual-Hard) is a family of two 200 -row surfaces. Both share the same twenty in-house algorithmic cores (reference implemen-tations plus fixed assert suites) and the same ten style indices per core ( 20 × 10 = 200 ). They differ only in which parts of the prompt are non-English. 

OMH-Wrapped (wrapper-only) keeps the tech-nical specification in English; each style index ap-plies one of ten hand-written multilingual or noisy wrappers (informal Turkish, Arabic, or Chinese openings; polite or chat-register English; Turkish– English mix; etc.). The cl100k_base per-prompt tokenization-overhead ratio is 0.96 × on the mean and 1.34 × at p90, so OMH-Wrapped cannot stress a fully non-English body. We retain it as a tokenizer control. 

OMH-Polyglot replaces the specification sen-tences themselves with Turkish, Arabic, Simplified Chinese, or code-switched equivalents; assert 

lines remain ASCII so the grader is unchanged. The twenty cores are interview-style tasks (e.g., 0/1

Knapsack, longest common subsequence, sliding-window maxima, topological sort, Levenshtein dis-tance). The ten style registers are: pure Turkish; pure Arabic (Modern Standard); pure Simplified Chinese; three pairwise sentence-alternating mixes (TR ↔AR, TR ↔ZH, AR ↔ZH); a tri-language TR/AR/ZH rotation; and three native-narrative reg-isters with embedded English jargon. The per-prompt tokenization-overhead ratio is 2.05 × on the mean, 4.02 × at p90, and 6.15 × in the worst case (Table 2).

4.2 Baselines and Implementation 

We evaluate against three commercial cloud backends spanning the cost–capability frontier: gpt-3.5-turbo , gpt-4o , and 

gemini-2.5-flash-lite . The local mid-dleware uses a headless OpenAI-compatible proxy running Llama 3.2 (3B) via Ollama ; only the upstream cloud endpoint changes. For the Raw 

arm, OckBench dispatches the multilingual prompt directly to the cloud API with a fixed system prompt asking for a focused Python solution. For the Ours arm, OckBench routes through our proxy; the system prompt is identical across both arms, so any delta is attributable to the user-message rewrite alone. We run three independent repeats per (model × pipeline) cell at temperature = 0 

to characterize residual API non-determinism, and report run means. All cloud calls use a 4096 -token output cap.

5.1 Prompt-Side and Answer-Side Behavior Prompt-side. The prompt-token column is the most consistent finding: within each backend, 

Ours reduces prompt tokens by 34 –47% , with run-to-run variation under 20 tokens out of nearly 

30 ,000 . Each backend uses a different tokenizer (cl100k_base , o200k_base , Gemini’s Sentence-Piece), so absolute counts are not cross-tokenizer commensurate; the defensible reading is that each tokenizer, applied to its own backend’s prompts, separately exhibits a large reduction. 

Answer-side. Only gemini-2.5-flash-lite 

produces shorter completions ( −8.9% ). OpenAI models produce longer completions: +3 .5% on 

gpt-4o and +42 .3% on gpt-3.5-turbo . The most plausible explanation is that the structurally clean Bi-Block removes hedging cues and elicits more complete implementations. The prompt-side savings absorb the answer-side increase in every case, so net total tokens still fall by 8.3–18 .8% .The middleware reliably compresses the input sur-face; it shapes the output surface only on certain backends. 

Accuracy on gpt-4o . The +1 .17 pp delta corre-sponds to a single task swinging in one of three Raw repeats, within the 1.15 pp run-level standard deviation; the names_only ablation (Section 5.2) confirms gpt-4o is essentially flat across arms. We do not claim it as a result. 

Optimization floor: benchmark artifact. 

Earlier preliminary experiments on OMH-Wrapped suggested an “Optimization Floor” for gpt-3.5-turbo . On OMH-Polyglot, no regression appears: accuracy is exactly 99 .50% 

in every Raw and Ours run. The names_only 

ablation sharpens this: with SLM rewrite stripped but the multilingual body kept, gpt-3.5-turbo 

drops to 79 .00% , a 20 .5 pp collapse. The earlier “floor” was a symptom of forwarding multilingual prompts to a weaker backend without translation. 

5.2 Ablation: Isolating the Function-Name Oracle 

The Ours arm bundles structural rewriting with a deterministic oracle that extracts target function identifiers from the assert lines and places them at the top of [TASK] . To attribute the accuracy column to a specific mechanism, we run names_only : the SLM is bypassed and the regex-extracted name is prepended to the raw multilingual prompt as a one-line English oracle. Three repeats per cell, identical otherwise (Table 4). The ablation cleanly separates two hypotheses. 

(i) The oracle alone is insufficient on weaker backends and harmful. It drops accuracy by 

20 .50 pp on gpt-3.5-turbo (99 .50 → 79 .00 ) and 

18 .50 pp on gemini-2.5-flash-lite (95 .00 →

76 .50 ); on gpt-4o it is neutral ( +0 .17 pp, within 

±1.32 pp). The mechanism is consistent with under-attention to the multilingual body once a salient English instruction is prepended: the model treats the prefix as authoritative and par-tially ignores the foreign-language text where edge cases and return semantics are encoded. (ii) The rewriter, not the oracle, is the active accu-racy mechanism. If the oracle drove the full 

delta, names_only would approximate full . It does not: full exceeds names_only by 20 .50 , 1.00 , and 

21 .50 pp on the three backends. The salience-only hypothesis is directly falsified, and the +3 .00 pp Gemini gain is attributable to the SLM rewrite. 

(iii) Gemini destabilizes into runaway genera-tion without scaffold. Under names_only , Gem-ini’s mean answer length explodes from 359 tokens (raw ) and 327 (full ) to 1,353 —a 3.8× expansion. A fraction of these answers are likely cap-truncated; even allowing for this, the lite model fails to termi-nate cleanly without English semantic scaffolding. This supports the structural-scaffold reading of (Ma et al., 2025). Two cells of the original five-condition matrix remain pending: regex_strip (a non-LLM pleas-antry stripper, bounding what deterministic cleanup achieves) and llm_no_names (rewriter with the ora-cle disabled, testing whether the oracle adds any-thing on top).

## 6 Conclusion

## 7 Limitations 

Pending ablations & baselines. regex_strip 

(non-LLM baseline) and llm_no_names (SLM without oracle) remain pending. While not required for our core accuracy claims, they would strengthen deployment guidance. Additionally, running Long-CodeZip (Shi et al., 2025) against a local reference LM, and sweeping LLMLingua-2 at r ∈ { 0.5, 0.7}

(currently fixed at r = 0 .6), are planned follow-ups to tighten Pareto contrasts. 

Statistical scope. OMH-Polyglot yields an effec-tive independent-trial count of ∼20 (20 clusters 

× 10 styles with shared solutions). Small accu-racy deltas ( ≤ 1.17 pp) fall within run-level API noise. While the large ablation drops ( −18 .50 to 

−20 .50 pp) are well outside this noise band, for-mal cluster-level bootstraps with Holm-corrected significance are required for inferential claims. 

Deployment overheads. The proxy adds 118 –

279 ms latency per prompt (Section 5.4), and the local Llama 3.2 (3B) requires ∼3.4 GB of mem-ory (AI, 2024). Furthermore, dollar-cost outcomes are asymmetric (Section 5.5): savings depend heav-ily on the backend’s output/input price ratio, with regressions on gpt-3.5-turbo (+15 .1% ) but sav-ings on gemini-2.5-flash-lite (−12 .4% ). 

Validation & scope mismatch. The post-rewrite validator (Section 3.4) performs syntactic checks but lacks semantic requirement-coverage tracking. Additionally, empirical results reflect the bench-mark proxy’s Bi-Block, whereas the production IDE uses an extended Tri-Block. Finally, Arabic and Chinese translations await native-speaker au-dits before external release. 

Compliance & Reproducibility. All models and datasets (Llama 3.2 (AI, 2024), LLMLingua-2 (Pan et al., 2024), OckBench (Du et al., 2026)) comply with their respective open-access licenses.
