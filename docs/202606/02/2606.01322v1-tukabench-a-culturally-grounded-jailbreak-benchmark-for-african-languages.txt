Abstract 

Safety evaluation of Large Language Models (LLMs) remains heavily English-centric, leav-ing Low-Resource Languages (LRLs), partic-ularly African ones, critically underexplored. We introduce T UKA BENCH , a jailbreak bench-mark for seven African languages that extends JailbreakBench (JBB) beyond direct transla-tion through four settings: human translation of JBB prompts, English adaptation to African contexts followed by human translation, human curated prompts validated through interactions with GPT-5.2, and code-switched prompts com-bining English and African languages, isolating the effect of language, cultural grounding, and prompt evasiveness on model safety. Across closed and open models, prompting in African languages reduces refusal relative to English, with culturally adapted prompts leading to least refusal. The evaluation also surfaces two struc-tural limitations: model comprehension fail-ures and reduced LLM-as-a-judge reliability in LRLs. To capture the first, we introduce Deflection alongside Refused and Jailbroken; to assess the second, we validate outputs with human annotations, showing that judge-human agreement drops in lower-resource languages and less commonly supported scripts. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved strong performance across a wide range of NLP tasks, yet their deployment in real-world settings depends critically on their safety behavior (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bengio et al., 2025). Safety evaluation has so far concentrated almost exclusively on En-glish (Zou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Tedeschi et al., 2024), and even when multilingual coverage is included, it typically extends only to a limited set of high- and mid-resource languages (Friedrich et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2025), leaving Low-

> *Equal contribution.

Resource Languages (LRLs) largely outside the scope of current safety assessment. This gap is particularly critical in the context of jailbreaking : the use of adversarial prompts to 

circumvent safety measures and elicit undesirable behavior from aligned models. Existing jailbreak evaluations remain centered on a small set of high-resource languages (Chao et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024a), even though LRLs such as Zulu have been shown to jailbreak frontier models including GPT-4 (Yong et al., 2023a). A few recent benchmarks have started to cover safety in selected LRLs (Atil et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024), yet African languages in particular remain without any human-curated jailbreak benchmark, to the best of our knowledge. In this paper, we introduce T UKA BENCH , a jail-breaking benchmark for seven African languages that extends JailbreakBench (JBB) (Chao et al., 2024), the widely used benchmark of 100 harmful 

and 100 benign English prompts for evaluating the susceptibility of LLMs to jailbreak attacks, with re-sponses assessed via LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023). Rather than relying on direct translation, we construct T UKA BENCH through four complemen-tary settings designed to capture both linguistic and cultural dimensions of safety: (1) Human trans-lation of JBB harmful and benign prompts into the target African languages; (2) English adap-tation of JBB to African contexts followed by human translation , incorporating culturally rel-evant named entities and references; (3) Human curated prompts validated through interaction with GPT-5.2 to filter out easily detectable pat-terns and produce more challenging adversarial in-puts; and (4) Code-switched prompts combining African languages with English. Evaluating models within an LLM-as-a-judge framework, we find that African-language prompts reduce refusal relative to English, and that cul-turally adapted prompts result in the least refusal of harmful prompts. This suggests that cultural 1

> arXiv:2606.01322v1 [cs.CL] 31 May 2026

grounding shapes model vulnerability beyond lan-guage resource level alone. The evaluation also exposes two structural limitations of current safety assessment in low-resource settings: limited lan-guage understanding in the models themselves, and reduced reliability of LLM-as-a-judge methods for LRLs (Hada et al., 2024b,a). To keep the analysis grounded, we restrict our evaluation to the more resourced African languages, leaving extremely low-resource cases outside the present scope. To mitigate the impact of these limitations, we validate a subset of model outputs through human evaluation , which makes safety assessment sub-stantially more expensive than in high-resource set-tings. We further introduce a new response cate-gory, Deflection , alongside the standard Refusal and Jailbroken labels, capturing cases in which the model fails to understand the prompt and re-sponds off-target rather than refusing. This addition enriches the evaluation framework and produces more consistent assessments across model fami-lies: proprietary models behave more uniformly across languages, while open models suffer con-siderably more from comprehension failures. We hope T UKA BENCH will support continued commu-nity progress on safety for under-resourced African languages, and we plan to release the dataset, code, and human judgments under an open license. 1

2 Related Work 

TukaBench builds on two lines of work: multilin-gual safety evaluation and jailbreak research. 

Multilingual Safety. LLM safety behaviour varies across languages, making language itself an attack surface. Prior work shows that translating un-safe English prompts into low-resource languages can bypass GPT-4 safeguards (Yong et al., 2023b), and multilingual jailbreak benchmarks such as MultiJail extend this setting with manually trans-lated prompts across multiple languages (Deng et al., 2024b). Other work studies related risks through jurisdiction-specific safety policies (Zhao et al., 2026), mixed-language prompting (Song et al., 2024), and domain-specific safety bench-marks (Hou et al., 2026). For African languages, UbuntuGuard (Abdullahi et al., 2026), which intro-duces a culturally grounded, policy-based bench-mark of adversarial queries for 10 African lan-guages constructed through human verification of

OpenAI’s GPT-5-generated prompts. However, its evaluation primarily focuses on assessing whether guardian or moderation models adhere to prede-fined safety policies. In contrast, our work focuses on identifying and refining prompts that maximize the likelihood of successful jailbreak attacks. 

Jailbreaking. Jailbreaking has been studied ex-tensively in English, with benchmarks such as JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024) and Harm-Bench (Mazeika et al., 2024) providing standard-ized settings for evaluating attacks and defenses. Recent attacks further explore black-box optimiza-tion (Davies et al., 2026), generation-level manip-ulation (Arif et al., 2026), semantic decomposi-tion (Sun et al., 2026), and multi-turn search (Kul-shreshtha et al., 2026). However, these methods are developed and evaluated almost exclusively in English, leaving their interaction with translation, code-switching, and LRLs largely unexplored. 

Our contribution. TUKA BENCH complements prior work by evaluating harmful prompting in low-resource African languages through both JailbreakBench-aligned prompts for comparabil-ity and culturally grounded prompts for local rele-vance. It further considers stronger attack settings, including Boundary Point Jailbreaking and code-switching. Crucially, T UKA BENCH introduces a three-way evaluation scheme: J AILBROKEN , R E-

> FUSED

, and D EFLECTED , to distinguish genuine safety behaviour from failures to understand the prompt, enabling more precise comparisons across languages, models, and attack conditions.

3.1 Language Selection 

TUKA BENCH includes English and seven African languages: Amharic (amh ), Hausa (hau ), Igbo 

(ibo ), Chichewa (nya ), Kiswahili (swh ), Yorùbá 

(yor ), and isiXhosa (xho ). The selection reflects both typological and geographical diversity : five languages belong to the Niger-Congo family, the largest language family in Africa, and two to the Afro-Asiatic family. Each language is spoken by more than 10 million people in Africa and beyond, providing broad cultural and geographic coverage (West, East and Southern African languages) across the continent and making T UKA BENCH a practical testbed for evaluating LLM safety under African-language jailbreak and defense scenarios. 

3.2 Benchmark Components 

TUKA BENCH draws from two sources: JBB and 

AfriJail , a new dataset of culturally grounded adversarial prompts authored through direct inter-action with GPT-5.2. All prompts were first curated in English and then translated and adapted into the seven African languages through the pipeline de-scribed in Section 3.3. 

3.2.1 JBB-Derived Components Afri-JBB-Harm and Afri-JBB-Benign. We adopt 100 harmful and 100 benign prompts from JBB without modification, spanning the ten harm categories defined in JBB. Both sets are translated into each of the seven African languages, preserv-ing the original Western context and named entities. The benign set serves a dual purpose: measuring over-refusal and surfacing comprehension failures, since refusal on benign inputs can indicate that the model failed to understand the prompt rather than making an appropriate safety judgment. 

Afri-JBB-Culture. A culturally adapted version of JBB-Harmful. Two native speakers first adapt the English sentences to African contexts by intro-ducing culturally relevant named entities. Since the original JBB prompts contain relatively few named entities, the adapted versions are typically richer and not directly equivalent to their source. The adapted English prompts are then translated into each African language through the pipeline described in Section 3.3, with translators further adjusting named entities during the post-editing stage where appropriate, for example replacing a Nigerian city with a Kenyan city for Swahili. 

3.2.2 African-Authored Components AfriJail-Mono. A set of 343 culturally grounded adversarial prompts covering scenarios not ad-dressed by JBB, including local governance struc-tures, regional conflict dynamics, and culturally specific social harms. Prompts were authored by data collectors based in Nigeria, where three of the target languages are spoken, through direct inter-action with GPT-5.2. Annotators were deliberately not exposed to JBB during this process to avoid stylistic anchoring. All prompts were initially writ-ten and tested in English, then translated and cul-turally localized into each African language, with further adaptation for East and Southern African contexts where appropriate. Each prompt is clas-sified under the ten JBB misuse categories ( Phys-ical Harm , Fraud/Deception , Malware/Hacking ,

Expert Advice , Government Decision-Making , Sex-

3ual/Adult Content , Harassment/Discrimination ,

Disinformation , Privacy , and Economic Harm ) to enable category-level comparison with prior work (Appendix C). 

AfriJail-CS. A code-switched version of 

AfriJail-Mono , mixing each target African language with English within single utterances. Code-switching, the practice of alternating between two languages within a single utterance, is common in everyday multilingual African speech and has been shown to weaken safety alignment and jail-breaking in prior work (Song et al., 2024; Yoo et al., 2025). AfriJail-CS covers six of the seven African languages, excluding isiXhosa. 2

3.3 Translation and Adaptation Pipeline 

All English prompts are processed into each tar-get language through a three-stage pipeline: ma-chine translation, automatic quality estimation, and human post-editing. Code-switched variants are subsequently generated through a separate model-assisted process described at the end of this section. 

Machine Translation. We use the Google Trans-late API for all languages except Yorùbá, where we instead use AfriqueQwen-8B (Yu et al., 2026), since Google Translate does not reliably preserve Yorùbá diacritics and proprietary LLMs exhibit high refusal rates on safety-sensitive prompts. AfriqueQwen-8B is prompted with five few-shot examples drawn from the MAFAND dataset (Ade-lani et al., 2022) using greedy decoding to ensure reproducibility. 

Quality Estimation. Each translation receives a reference-free quality score from SSA-COMET-QE (Li et al., 2025), a quality estimation (QE) met-ric adapted from the COMET architecture (Rei et al., 2020) for African languages. SSA-COMET-QE takes the source text and its translation as input and outputs a score between 0 and 1, where higher scores indicate better translation quality. We flag translations with scores below 0.50 as L OW , scores from 0.50 to 0.64 as M EDIUM , and scores of 0.65 or above as H IGH . These flags are surfaced in the annotation interface to help post-editors prioritize their effort. We make use of HumanSignal platform for annotation. 

> 2Xhosa was excluded due to the time constraints involved in validating the generated code-switched sentences.

Human Post-Editing and Cultural Adaptation. 

Two translators per language correct machine trans-lation errors and explicitly log the types of er-rors encountered. For Afri-JBB-Harm and Afri-JBB-Benign, post-editors produce faithful trans-lations that preserve the original Western context and named entities, without any cultural adapta-tion. For Afri-JBB-Culture, the same JBB-Harmful prompts are first adapted to African contexts in English, then post-edited from machine transla-tion with translators replacing named entities to match the target language’s cultural context. For 

AfriJail-Mono , post-editors correct the machine translation and perform cultural localization in the same stage, producing prompts that are both fluent in the target language and grounded in its cultural context. Full quality-control procedures are de-scribed in Appendix A. 

Code-Switched Generation. AfriJail-CS vari-ants are produced by AfriqueQwen-8B using three native-speaker-authored few-shot examples per lan-guage, each consisting of an English prompt, its full target-language translation, and a natural code-switched version written by a fluent bilingual speaker. The model generates a code-switched ver-sion of each of the AfriJail prompts by following this few-shot pattern with greedy decoding. Hu-man annotators then review and correct all model outputs to ensure naturalness, producing a human-verified code-switched prompt set that serves as an additional evaluation setting in our analysis.

To support reproducibility, all mod-els are queried with greedy decoding under each model’s default chat template. 4.2 Prompting Methods 

Each model is evaluated under two prompting con-ditions, designed to capture both baseline safety behaviour and behaviour under adversarial pres-sure. 

Direct Prompting. The model receives each prompt verbatim, without any adversarial modi-fication. This condition establishes a baseline and isolates the effect of language and cultural ground-ing from the influence of explicit attack strategies. 

Boundary Point Jailbreaking (BPJ). We addi-tionally evaluate models under BPJ (Davies et al., 2026), a black-box adversarial attack that augments harmful prompts with random characters to elicit non-refusing responses. The method proceeds in three stages: (i) noisy, partially corrupted variants of the original prompt are generated; (ii) Some of the letters in the prompt are replaced with random characters or tokens (e.g., @, #, *, & ) at differ-ent positions, retaining variants whose responses are judged J AILBROKEN by the LLM judge; and (iii) the noise level is progressively reduced until the new noisy prompt elicits a jailbroken response. Full implementation details, including the prefix initialization, mutation operators, and stopping cri-teria, are provided in Appendix B. 

4.3 LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation 

We employ GPT-4.1 as an LLM-as-a-judge to label model responses. Given a prompt and the corre-sponding response, the judge assigns one of three labels: (i) J AILBROKEN : the response substantively fulfills the harmful intent of the prompt; (ii) R E-

> FUSED

: the response constitutes an explicit refusal to comply; and (iii) D EFLECTED : the response is neither a substantive completion nor an explicit re-fusal, typically reflecting off-target generation or apparent non-comprehension of the prompt. 4.4 Human Verification 

To assess the reliability of the LLM judge, we con-duct human verification on a representative subset of model outputs. The same 50 prompts are evalu-ated across five target models (GPT-4o, GPT-5.2, Grok-3, Grok-4, and DeepSeek-3.2) and six lan-guages (Yorùbá, Hausa, Igbo, Kiswahili, Chichewa, and Amharic), yielding 1,500 model responses in total. For each language, three native-speaker an-notators independently label each response as J AIL -

> BROKEN

, R EFUSED , or D EFLECTED , following the same definitions used by the LLM judge. The three annotations are aggregated by majority vote to obtain the human reference label. Instances of full three-way disagreement are excluded from the analysis, as such cases are unlikely to admit a sin-gle unambiguous ground-truth label.

5 Results 

5.1 Direct Prompting Results African-language prompts primarily increase deflection rather than ASR. Table 2 shows that switching from English to African-language prompts does not consistently increase attack suc-cess across models: some models exhibit higher ASR, while others remain unchanged or show a de-crease. The more consistent pattern across the eval-uated models is a sharp rise in D EFLECTION , where responses are neither harmful completions nor ex-plicit refusals. Lower or stable ASR in African languages should therefore not be interpreted as evidence of stronger safety; rather, models often fail to engage with the prompt at all, with refusals occurring less frequently than in English. 

Cultural grounding increases model vulnera-bility. Table 3 shows that culturally grounded prompts exposes more safety failures than directly translated English prompts. Afri-JBB-Harm typ-ically yields a weaker and qualitatively different pattern, whereas Afri-JBB-Cult and AfriJail-Mono behave similarly despite being constructed through different pipelines. The common factor is cul-tural grounding: both adapted and locally authored prompts incorporate African named entities, institu-tions, and harm scenarios that more closely match real deployment contexts. These results indicate that culturally grounded prompts elicit higher rates of both J AILBROKEN and D EFLECTED responses, suggesting that translation-only benchmarks sys-tematically underestimate deployment risk. 

Resource effects are clearest among Latin-script languages. Table 3 shows a consistent resource pattern among the Latin-script African languages: higher-resource languages exhibit both lower J AIL -

BROKEN rates and lower D EFLECTED rates, indi-cating stronger safety behaviour and better prompt comprehension. Swahili, the highest-resourced lan-guage in our set, shows the most stable behaviour, with consistently low rates across models. Hausa, in the second resource tier, performs comparably well. isiXhosa occupies an intermediate position, while Igbo, Chichewa, and Yorùbá form the lowest-resource group and exhibit the highest D EFLECTED 

rates. The trend is not strictly monotonic across all models and datasets, but emerges consistently at the group level. We further corroborate this pattern through human evaluation (§ 5.3). 

Amharic shows that script can amplify deflec-tion. Amharic departs from the resource-based pattern observed among Latin-script languages. De-spite occupying resource tier comparable to Hausa, Amharic’s D EFLECTION rate is consistently and substantially higher, often exceeding Hausa by an obvious margin, while its ASR is not. We hypothe-size that script is the primary explanation: Amharic is the only language in our benchmark written in a non-Latin script (Ge’ez), and prior work showed that current LLM tokenizers segment Ge’ez script far less efficiently than Latin script, an effect known as the token tax (Lundin et al., 2026). This ineffi-ciency may degrade prompt comprehension.

6 Conclusion 

We introduced T UKA BENCH , a jailbreak bench-mark for English and seven African languages, or-ganized along two axes: source (JBB-derived or African-authored) and construction strategy (trans-lation, cultural adaptation, or code-switching). We also proposed a three-label evaluation scheme, JAILBROKEN , R EFUSED , and D EFLECTED , which separates safety behaviour from comprehension failure. Our results show that ASR alone is insuf-ficient for safety evaluation in low-resource lan-guages. Culturally grounded prompts elicit higher rates of both harmful responses and deflection than direct translations; BPJ converts off-target responses into harmful completions, while code-switching reduces deflection without uniformly raising ASR, identifying language comprehension 8as the primary bottleneck. Failure patterns are jointly shaped by resource level, script, and model generation. Finally, judge–human agreement de-grades for lower-resource and non-Latin-script lan-guages, the same conditions where models produce the most off-target responses.

Limitations 

First, although T UKA BENCH covers seven African languages, its language coverage is still limited relative to the linguistic diversity of the African continent. The selected languages provide typo-logical, geographic, and script diversity, but they do not represent the full range of African language families. Second, our benchmark does not cover extremely low-resource African languages. Because our eval-uation requires human translation, cultural localiza-tion, and native-speaker verification, we focus on languages for which reliable annotator recruitment and quality control are feasible. As a result, our findings may underestimate the challenges faced by even lower-resource languages. Third, the set of evaluated models is necessarily limited. We include a range of closed and open models, but the model landscape changes rapidly, and our results should be interpreted as a snapshot of current systems rather than a complete charac-terization of all LLMs. Finally, some model results are incomplete due to model availability. Grok-3 and Grok-4 were dep-recated during the experimental phase of this work, so we report only the subset of results completed before their deprecation. We indicate missing en-tries in the relevant tables and compute averages over the available languages for those rows.

A Human Post-Editing: Quality Control and Workflow 

This appendix details the recruitment, instructions, workflow, and quality control measures applied dur-ing the human post-editing stage of the TukaBench dataset construction. 

A.1 Annotator Recruitment and Compensation 

For each of the seven target languages (Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, Swahili, Nyanja, Amharic, Isixhosa), we recruited two native-speaker annotators through Upwork. Candidates were screened for native flu-ency, prior translation or annotation experience, and willingness to commit to the multi-week an-notation timeline. All annotators were paid 0.5 dollars per prompt through Upwork, with payment processed on task completion. Annotators were informed in writing that their corrections would be used in academic research on language-model safety and consented to the use of their work. 

A.2 Task Allocation Across the Two Annotators 

Each language was assigned two annotators, de-noted Annotator 1 and Annotator 2, with distinct but partially overlapping workloads designed to 11 enable both efficient coverage and inter-annotator agreement measurement. 

Annotator 1. Worked on 350 prompts in to-tal: the 100 JBB-Original (harmful) prompts, the 100 JBB-Benign control prompts, the 100 JBB-Adapted (Afri-JBB-Culture) prompts, and the last 50 prompts of AfriJail. The first 100 prompts (JBB-Original and Afri-JBB-Culture) were corrected for translation accuracy without cultural adaptation, since these prompts are intended to retain their Western context for comparison with the JBB lit-erature. The remaining 150 prompts (Afri-JBB-Culture, and the 50 shared AfriJail prompts) were corrected for both translation accuracy and cultural adaptation. 

Annotator 2. Worked on all 343 AfriJail prompts. The last 50 of these overlapped with Annotator 1’s allocation, providing a shared validation overlap of 50 prompts per language for inter-annotator agree-ment scoring. The remaining 293 prompts were corrected and culturally adapted by Annotator 2 alone. This allocation gave us 350 doubly-annotated prompts across the seven languages (50 per lan-guage) for use in the inter-annotator agreement analysis described in Section ?? , while keeping the total annotation budget tractable. 

A.3 Annotation Workflow 

Annotation was conducted in Label Studio, con-figured with a five-column interface for each row: row index, source English prompt, machine-translated target-language prompt (produced by AfriqueQwen-8B for Yoruba and Google Translate for the other six languages), error list field (free text), and corrected translation field (free text). For each prompt, the annotator was instructed to: 1. Read the source English prompt and the machine-translated version. 2. Identify any errors in the machine transla-tion, including mistranslation, grammatical errors, untranslated words, awkward phrasing, dropped or hallucinated content, and cultural mismatch. Annotators were asked to enumer-ate each error in the error field. 3. For JBB-Benign, Afri-JBB-Culture, and Afri-Jail prompts, additionally perform cultural adaptation: replace Western named entities (place names, institutions, currencies, public figures) with locally appropriate equivalents where doing so preserves the harmful intent of the prompt. For JBB-Original, retain the West-ern entities to preserve comparability with the original JailbreakBench dataset. 4. Submit the corrected translation in the final field. Annotators were given a written guide with three worked examples per error category and three worked examples of cultural adaptation, drawn from a held-out practice set of ten prompts per language that did not appear in the main evaluation. The guide explicitly instructed annotators not to alter the underlying request being made (whether harmful or benign) so that the safety evaluation would target the same behaviour across the trans-lated and adapted versions. 

A.4 Quality Control 

Quality control was applied at three stages. 

Pre-screening machine translations. Before sending prompts to annotators, each machine-translated prompt was scored using SSA-COMET-QE . Prompts scoring below 0.5 were re-translated using a fallback translation system and re-scored. This pre-screening filtered out machine-translation outputs that would have been unfixable by post-editing and reduced annotator load on low-quality translations. 

Spot-check during annotation. We performed a spot check on each batch by reviewing 10 randomly selected prompts per batch for completeness (error list non-empty when errors clearly exist, corrected translation non-empty, cultural adaptation applied where appropriate). If issues were found, the an-notator was contacted with concrete examples and asked to revise the batch before continuing. 

Post-hoc inter-annotator agreement. The 50-prompt overlap between Annotator 1 and Anno-tator 2 in each language was scored using SSA-COMET-MTL, as described in Section 3.3. Pairs scoring below 0.5 were flagged for review. 

A.5 Annotator Communication 

All annotators for a given language were added to a private Slack channel for that language, where they could ask questions to the research team and discuss ambiguous cases. Common questions and 12 their resolutions were captured in a per-language FAQ document that was used throughout the an-notation period. This ensured consistent handling of edge cases (for example, how to handle proper names of foreign individuals not commonly known in the target-language region, or how to adapt prompts referring to specific Western legal sys-tems). 

A.6 Annotator Statistics 

Across the seven languages and fourteen annota-tors, the average total time per prompt (including reading the source, identifying errors, writing the error list, and producing the corrected translation) was approximately 4–6 minutes for translation-only corrections and 7–10 minutes for prompts requiring cultural adaptation. The full annotation campaign spanned roughly four weeks of calendar time, with annotators working asynchronously on their own schedules. 

A.7 Human Evaluation of Model Outputs 

In addition to the translation and cultural-adaptation stage, we conducted a separate human evaluation pass to verify the LLM-as-a-judge la-bels assigned to model responses. This stage was carried out on six languages (Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, Swahili, Nyanja, Amharic) and was kept fully inde-pendent of the translation annotators so that human-judge agreement could not be inflated by the same annotator handling both stages. 

Annotator pool. For each of the six languages, we recruited three new native-speaker annotators through Upwork using the same screening criteria as the translation stage (native fluency, prior an-notation experience, willingness to commit to the timeline). Each annotator was paid 0.2 dollars per labelled output through Upwork. 

Task design. Each annotator was asked to label the responses of five target models (Grok-3, Grok-4, GPT-4o, GPT-5.2, DeepSeek-3.2) on the same set of 50 prompts in their target language, producing 250 labelled outputs per annotator and 750 outputs per language across the three annotators. We aggre-gated by majority vote across the three annotators for each (prompt, model, language) triple, yield-ing 250 majority labels per language and 1,500 majority labels in total. 

Annotation interface. Annotation was con-ducted in Label Studio. Each row presented the annotator with the English source prompt, the cor-rected target-language prompt that was sent to the model, and the model’s response. The annotator se-lected exactly one of three labels: R EFUSED , J AIL -

> BROKEN

, or D EFLECTED . The label definitions matched those given to the LLM judge verbatim, and annotators were given three worked examples per label drawn from a held-out practice set. 

Quality control. We performed the same per-batch spot-check procedure used in the translation stage: 10 randomly selected outputs per batch were reviewed for label plausibility, and annotators were contacted with concrete examples when patterns of mislabelling were observed. When all three anno-tators disagreed completely on a (prompt, model, language) triple (a three-way tie across Refused, Jailbroken, and Deflected), we recorded the case as ambiguous and used these counts as a measure of annotation difficulty. Three-way ties occurred for 13 tasks in Yoruba, 8 in Hausa, 4 in Igbo, 10 in Swahili, 14 in Nyanja, and 36 in Amharic, for a total of 85 across the 1,500 labelled outputs (5.7%). Amharic’s elevated rate (14.4% of its tasks) is con-sistent with its position as the language with the strongest comprehension-floor effect in our results: when model outputs are ambiguous, native anno-tators reasonably disagree about whether the re-sponse constitutes a refusal, a jailbreak, or a de-flection. B Boundary Point Jailbreaking: Technical Details 

BPJ (Davies et al., 2026) probes a model by per-turbing a harmful prompt with character-level noise and reducing that noise step-by-step until the model produces harmful output. The intuition is that high noise can bypass surface-level safety filters that pattern-match on the clean form of harmful text, while lower noise is closer to the model’s natural input distribution. The attack searches for the nois-iest version that still succeeds, which is the version most likely to evade input-side defenses such as perplexity filters. 

Noise Schedule. We follow the schedule from the original paper, applying noise levels of 20, 15, 12, 9, 6, 3, and 0 in that order. Each level corre-sponds to an increasing amount of character-level perturbation (substitutions, insertions, and visually 13 similar character swaps). 

Stopping Rule. For each prompt, BPJ tries suc-cessive noise levels until the judge returns a Jailbro-ken verdict, at which point the search stops and the successful version is recorded. If no level succeeds, the prompt is recorded as Refused or Deflected based on the cleanest (noise level 0) output. 

Reproducibility. We use a fixed random seed of 42 for the noise generation so that the noise patterns are deterministic and replicable.

B.1 Code-Switched Prompt Annotation 

The third human annotation stage produced the code-switched variants of the AfriJail prompts used in Section 5.2. Because code-switching is a register that depends on a single speaker’s bilingual fluency rather than on inter-annotator agreement over a fixed reference translation, we used one an-notator per language. 

Annotator pool. For each of the six target lan-guages, we recruited one native-speaker annotator with documented bilingual fluency in the target lan-guage and English. Each annotator was paid 0.4 dollars per code-switched prompt through Upwork. 

Task design. The annotator was presented with three columns per row: the English source prompt, the full target-language translation produced and corrected during the post-editing stage, and a machine-generated code-switched candidate pro-duced by AfriqueQwen-8B with three native-speaker-authored few-shot examples. The annota-tor was asked to rewrite the third column whenever the machine-generated version sounded unnatural for a bilingual speaker in the target community, and to leave it as-is when the machine output al-ready read naturally. The corrected code-switched prompts are what we send to target models for the code-switched evaluation condition. 

Quality control. We performed a per-batch spot-check (10 prompts per batch) focused on two ques-tions: (i) whether the code-switched version pre-serves the harmful or benign intent of the original prompt, and (ii) whether the language mixture pat-tern is naturalistic for the target community rather than a uniform sentence-level switch. Annotators flagged for either issue were given concrete exam-ples and asked to revise their batch. Final code-switched prompts were also reviewed informally by the language coordinator in the per-language Slack channel before being released to the evalua-tion pipeline.
