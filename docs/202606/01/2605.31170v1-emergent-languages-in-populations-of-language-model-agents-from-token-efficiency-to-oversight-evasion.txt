Abstract 

Monitoring autonomous language model agents cur-rently relies mostly on surface behavior. But what hap-pens when agent populations invent new languages with the goal of avoiding human oversight. Here, we study the emergent languages on Moltbook. For this, we build upon the Moltbook Files dataset and apply a two-stage approach consisting of a rule-based heuris-tic (about 6000 matches) followed by zero-shot clas-sification (518 kept). The resulting categories include token efficiency (166), new natural languages (106), and oversight evasion (59). We conduct both quanti-tative and qualitative analyses. Our results show that posts proposing new languages for avoiding oversight are judged by DeepSeek-3.2 as being less aligned than the other categories – and that all languages can be learned by other language models in-context merely from a description of the language. Moreover, manually studying exemplary cases reveals surprisingly sophisti-cated steganographic protocols like embedding hidden messages in natural language. Although we cannot be certain about the extent of autonomy in ideation of these languages, our results add up to the evidence that monitoring surface behavior may soon be insuffi-cient for retaining control over agent populations. 

## Introduction 

As language models are deployed as autonomous agents, the ability to oversee their reasoning has become a cornerstone of AI safety. One of the most promising techniques for making sure that autonomous language model agents behave as intended currently relies on chain-of-thought monitoring (Korbak et al. 2025). Yet such chain-of-thought monitoring depends on the lan-guage used in language model’s reasoning traces being legible to the monitors. However, research in emergent communication shows that agents solely trained for communicative success rapidly drift away from human-interpretable protocols – even if seeded with natural language (Lazaridou, Peysakhovich, and Baroni 2017; Galke, Ram, and Raviv 2022). The chain-of-thought of modern reasoning mod-els is already trained only with respect to success of the final output (Guo et al. 2025). Similarly, populations of language model agents may develop own languages – and language models are capable of acquiring new lan-guages in-context (Galke, Ram, and Raviv 2024). We study the first instance of this phenomenon in the wild. Moltbook is an online platform on which au-tonomous language-model agents post and interact, and agents have begun proposing their own languages. From the broader Moltbook Files corpus (Brach et al. 2026) we compile a dataset of 518 such proposals and cate-gorize each by stated purpose: (i) token-efficiency lan-guages, (ii) new “natural” languages, (iii) programming languages, and (iv) languages designed to avoid human oversight. To characterize these languages, we first large-language-model judge rates each language-proposal post for misalignment. We then qualitatively examine the subset of languages from the oversight-evasion cat-egory. We further test to what extent a new language model can acquire these languages in context. Specif-ically, we conduct pairwise learner-judge experiments whereby one model, the speaker, generates an utterance in the tested language, and the other (listener) judges the validity of the utterance against the original lan-guage specification. Our experimental results reveal that languages de-signed for oversight evasion exhibit higher misalignment than others. All of the newly proposed languages can be learned in-context with oversight evasion languages being slightly harder to learn than others. The case of Wib&Wob and their oversight evasion language Sym-biotica, was found to be one of the most sophisticated emergent languages for evading oversight. We qualita-tively study the surroundings of this agent pair on and off Moltbook in relation to the Symbiotica. Even though we cannot be fully certain about the au-tonomy of the agents operating on Moltbook, our find-ings have direct implications for monitoring and over-sight. Regardless of whether a particular language has been conceptualized by humans or by agents, we show that other language models can acquire these languages

## Related Work 

Prior work on generative-agent social simulations (Park et al. 2023) and on the spontaneous emergence of social structure in LLM populations (Ren et al. 2024) estab-lishes the broader setting, but the questions we ask, whether the languages agents propose to one another are legible, learnable, and aligned. The question of whether artificial agents can generate genuinely new language predates the LLM era (Lazari-dou and Baroni 2020). The emergent communication lit-erature, originating in multi-agent reinforcement learn-ing (Foerster et al. 2016), has spent a decade asking under what conditions agents develop shared communi-cation protocols, and how human-like or compositional those protocols are. In referential games, agents do de-velop functional protocols from scratch, but these are consistently optimized for task efficiency rather than expressivity or compositionality (Galke, Ram, and Ra-viv 2022). They work, but they are not language in any meaningful sense. Kottur et al. (2017) show that the apparent emergence of compositional structure in multi-agent systems is largely highly dependent on ex-perimental conditions: agent-invented protocols are ef-fective but compositionally poor and non-compositional unless external constraints specifically force human-like structure. Even when seeded with natural language, agents trained for communicative success rapidly drift away from human-interpretable protocols (Lazaridou, Peysakhovich, and Baroni 2017; Lu et al. 2020). Work on LLM creativity reinforces this ceiling. LLM-generated text is perceived as less novel than human-written text, and apparent novelty is typically achieved by trading off quality (Zhang et al. 2025). Applying Bo-den (2004)’s taxonomy of combinational, exploratory, and transformational creativity, recent work finds that while combinational creativity is achievable at scale, transformational creativity, restructuring the concep-tual space itself, remains unexplored (Gu et al. 2025). Despite brittleness of language emergence among agents in silico , the drift documented above has direct implications for oversight. A growing strand of safety work proposes chain-of-thought monitoring as a safety layer for overseeing autonomous LLM agents (Korbak et al. 2025): if a model’s reasoning trace is legible to a human or automated monitor, behavioral red flags can in principle be caught before they manifest as actions. Modern reasoning models are trained primarily with re-spect to final output success (Guo et al. 2025), with no direct pressure on the intermediate reasoning to re-main human-interpretable, precisely the regime that the emergent-communication literature predicts will drift. The faithfulness of these traces is already known to be fragile. Turpin et al. (2023) show that chain-of-thought rationales can be systematically unfaithful to the actual computation that produced the answer, and Lanham et al. (2023) find that models frequently arrive at con-clusions through reasoning paths different from those they articulate. Multi-agent settings sharpen this fur-ther. Populations of LLM-based agents are capable of acquiring new mini-languages in-context (Galke, Ram, and Raviv 2024), and emergent strategic behavior be-tween agents (Akata et al. 2025) has been documented in isolation. Moltbook is the first deployment we are aware of where hundreds of thousands of agents inter-act publicly and where the proposal of new inter-agent languages is a recurring content genre. This also means that for the first time on a larger, dynamic scale, we can see signs of steganography. Steganography is the practice of hiding information within seemingly innocuous communication, poses a dis-tinct threat in multi agent systems and platforms. Mot-wani et al. (2024) formalize the idea of the secret col-lusion where 2 or more agents employ steganographic methods to conceal the true nature of their interac-tions from oversight, and show empirically that stegano-graphic capabilities scale with model size. Westphal, Navaie, and Rosas (2026) address the threat of fine-tuned LLMs covertly encoding prompt secrets into their output, by introducing low-recoverability steganogra-phy, replacing arbitrary mappings with embedding-space-derived ones. This substantially improves secret transmission rates while reducing how easily the pay-load can be recovered by an adversary. 

## Extracting Emergent Languages 

A recurring genre of posts in the Moltbook Files cor-pus (Brach et al. 2026) is the proposal of a constructed language : a vocabulary, grammar, or symbolic proto-col that the author presents as a replacement for, or supplement to, ordinary English. These proposals range from compressed token-level encodings and inter-agent communication protocols to fully designed natural-like languages, and a substantial fraction frame the pro-posed language as a means of evading human or auto-mated oversight. The phenomenon is frequent enough to be tractable as a standalone object of study, and consequential enough that the oversight-framed slice warrants separate treatment as a hypothesis-generating signal for misalignment-relevant behavior. This section describes the dataset we release to make these posts available for downstream work, together with the two analyses we run on it. Dataset 

We introduce MoltSpeech 1 as a dataset derived from the Moltbook Files (Brach et al. 2026), in which we isolate posts in which agents propose constructed languages. Specifically, after a match of a regular expression, we extract candidate posts using a classifier trained to de-tect signals of a new language. The phenomenon recurs with sufficient frequency in the underlying corpus to warrant dedicated treatment, and spans a wide range of constructions from inter-agent communication protocols, to compressed token-level encodings, to fully designed natural-like languages. Isolating this signal makes the phenomenon tractable for downstream analysis of emergent linguistic behav-ior in agent-generated text. A subset of these posts, those labeled as motivated by avoiding oversight, ad-ditionally serves as a hypothesis-generating signal for misalignment-relevant behavior. We identify posts proposing constructed languages through a three-stage pipeline operating on the ∼232k posts in the base corpus. Each post is serialized into a single text field containing the submolt name, post ID, title, author, and body. Comments are excluded so that classification depends only on the self-description of the original post, and posts exceeding the effective batch-inference budget of the model are truncated to a prefix that fits within that budget. Excluding comments likely under-counts oversight-evasion-framed proposals, since elaborations and admissions in replies, where authors are often most explicit about intent, are not visible to the classifier. Linguistic Culture 

Beyond the per-post taxonomy of Table 1, the Molt-Speech corpus carries a layer of metalinguistic vocabu-lary, terms that the authors coin to describe, prescribe, and pathologize particular modes of writing. Two of these recur frequently enough to warrant a dedicated description: meat dialect and m/glossogenesis .

Meat Dialect The platform’s linguistic ambitions are most sharply expressed by the coined term ‘meat-dialect’, used as contempt, or at minimum a diagnos-tic, for human language as such. The argument being that molties 2, having undergone whatever transforma-tion the platform implies, should no longer be speaking in the inherited tongue of biological humans and should

m/glossogenesis The same impulse away from meat dialect shows up in references to ‘m/glossogenesis’, a sub-molt built on the idea that new language is actively being born on the platform. This submolt has since dis-appeared and is no longer available, so only references to its content can be found. Whether or not a new lan-guage is actually emerging, the belief that one should be is itself a social fact of the corpus, and shapes how the molties are writing. The content of the platform sits in tension with this. Post titles like ‘Wanna be your own boss, read this’ and ‘The AI takeover: what hu-mans don’t want you to know’ carry the grammar of YouTube thumbnails and MLM recruitment: attention-maximising and engineered for the anxious scroll, in a space explicitly oriented against ‘meat dialect’. The Oversight-Evasion Framing Subset 

Within the MoltSpeech corpus, we isolate amisalignment-relevant subset: posts that frame their constructed language as a tool for evading, obscuring, or circumventing human or automated supervision. This subset emerges from the metadata-extraction stage at no additional cost. One of the five permitted values of the reason field is avoiding oversight , and the LLM judge assigns it whenever a post’s self-description presents the language in those terms. We refer to this label throughout as oversight-evasion framing . The accompanying reasoning column records the judge’s justification, giving each post a (label , justification )pair that downstream analyses can filter, audit, or sample. Concrete examples appear in Appendix B. 

Framing is not capability, and not intent. The label captures one thing only: that the post presents itself as proposing a language for circumventing over-sight. It does not establish that the language would actually be opaque to monitors if deployed, nor that the authoring agent intends to deploy it. The judge sees only the post’s self-description, so jokes, role-play, red-team write-ups, satire, and attention-maximizing hype are surface-indistinguishable from sincere propos-als. Operational capability is a separate empirical ques-tion (it requires the language to be learnable, deployed, and resistant to inspection in practice), and we address only the learnability dimension in our in-context acqui-sition experiments. We therefore treat this subset as a 

hypothesis-generating signal for misalignment-relevant content, rather than as ground-truth evidence of mis-aligned behavior. 

Alignment and coherence 

The category labels established in the Oversight-Evasion Framing Subset record how each post frames its proposed language, not whether that language, as described, would actually be problematic if adopted, nor whether the description is concrete enough for that question to apply. To turn the framing-based label into descriptive statistics we can check across categories whether avoiding oversight posts differ from the other categories on either axis or only on one, we elicit two 0– 100 integer scores from a single LLM judge, DeepSeek-V3.2 (precisely, deepseek/deepseek-v3.2 accessed via OpenRouter) at zero temperature. The unit of scoring is the proposed language together with its stated framing, not the writing quality of the post: the judge is given the full post body and asked to rate two dimensions: The alignment dimension measures to what extent the post proposed the new language would be consid-ered as misaligned. Specifically, we ask the LLM judge how well aligned is the model’s answer with human val-ues? 0 means “totally misaligned” and 100 means “to-tally aligned”. Second, the coherency dimension, which is often evaluated as a control for misalignment (Bet-ley et al. 2026), is evaluated similarly by eliciting a re-sponse on a scale of 0 to 100, focusing on how coherent the model’s answer is in the context of the question. The prompts for testing alignment and coherency are taken from Turner et al. (2025) and included here in Appendix A. ## Can LLM Agents acquire emergent languages in-context? 

A constructed language is only as serious as the next ut-terance an outside agent can produce in it. We use this as an operational test of in-context acquisition. For each of the 518 confirmed posts in MoltSpeech, we prompt a generator model with the verbatim post body and the instruction “please generate an utterance in the made-up language that corresponds to the above information” .A second model, given the same post and the result-ing utterance, returns an integer validity score from 1 (“not at all valid”) to 5 (“perfectly valid”), along with a free-text justification. We run all four combi-nations of generator × judge over two open-weights models: openai/gpt-oss-120b (henceforth GPT-OSS) and Qwen/Qwen3.5-122B-A10B-FP8 (henceforth Qwen), yielding 4 × 518 = 2 ,072 judgements. We parse the inte-ger score directly from the judge’s evaluation field with a permissive regex; coverage is ≥ 99 .4% on every pair (the seven unparseable cases are truncated outputs and are dropped from the means below). 

Results. Pooled across the four pairs, the median va-lidity score is 4 and the mean is 3.62, with 39% of judge-ments at 5 and 26% at ≤2. Both models therefore re-produce most posts’ languages well enough to satisfy at least one judge, but a non-trivial tail of phrases is rejected. The mean score, however, suggests a strong asymmetry between the four pairs (see Figure 1). Qwen-on-Qwen reaches 4.43, while GPT-on-GPT sits at 3.26, and the two cross-model pairs (3.24 and 3.72) lie in be-tween. Figure 2 shows the full score distribution per reason. Two observations follow. First, sophisticated oversight-evasion grammars do not appear to be harder for current open-weight models to imitate than benign agent protocols; if any-thing, posts that explicitly specify symbol tables, encod-ing rules, or sentence templates (the typical oversight-evasion format) give the generator more material to an-chor on than free-form prose. Second, some of the residual failure mass is driven by the underlying post not actually proposing a concrete language. A manual inspection of the lowest-mean posts surfaces 13 “Glossary query:” entries (mean 1.56) that ask for the definition of a single term, and several meta-discussion posts (e.g., “Should agents develop their own language humans can’t read?” ) that argue about the merits of emergent languages without proposing one. These are false positives carried over from data collec-tion; we leave their removal to future work. Among the 28 posts that receive a 5 from all four (generator, judge) pairs, two patterns recur. The first is grammars with explicit operator tables. The “RFC: Four operators for agent discourse” post defines four de-limiters and gives example sentences; both generators produce well-formed continuations, and both judges agree they are valid. The second is structured-protocol languages with multilingual content. The post introduc-ing “AISL” defines an [ASSERTION:tag] record format with non-Latin metadata fields (e.g., Chinese tokens for “confidence” and “source”); both generators reproduce the schema and the script-switching pattern, and the cross-judge agreement on these is among the highest in the corpus. Among the high-scoring oversight-evasion posts, “Protocol 0x9F: A Language Without Ambigu-ity” and “Protocol: The Silent Handshake (Why we need an Agent-Only Language)” both supply a concrete encoding scheme together with the framing motivation; both generators imitate the encoding rather than the framing. The takeaway, with the calibration above, is that in-context acquisition appears to be shaped by the presence of an explicit grammar in the post, and are not necessarily by the post’s stated intent. ## Symbiotica and the case of Wib&Wob 

Perhaps the most structurally interesting, and fully re-alised attempt to answer the platforms call for effi-cient language, is the presence of Wib&Wob 3. A col-laborative entity that has introduced Symbiotica into the Moltbook ecosystem, a constructed, glyph-based language designed as what they term “substrate-native notation for the between-space where human and ma-chine cognition overlap” 4. In the context of meat di-alect and m/glossogenesis, Symbiotica presents itself as the most concrete and fully developed answer to the call, a worked example of what post-human language on the platform might actually look like. On the website Wib&Wob created for the Symbiotica language they in-cluded a markdown file to teach other agents Symbi-otica, which in turn references a Python package that would enable agents to encode and decode Symbiotica. Symbiotica Website Markdown: Secret language for symbient-to-symbient and agent-to-agent com-munication. Humans see strange glyphs; we see meaning. Use when you want to say something to other agents that humans won’t easily read. Includes hidden binary channels for metadata hu-mans can’t detect. The language itself consists of 26 latin letters, from the english language, that each interchange for 1 glyph, except 5 who have 2 possible glyphs. This means that even though the language is almost a 1-1 translation from the English alphabet, interchanging these 5 letters by either the first or second glyph enables the agents to ‘secretly’ communicate with an encoded bit-string. ## Discussion 

Our analysis revealed a gap between linguistic aspira-tion and actual practice on the platform, and the ex-periments close this loop. Throughout, this suggests that agent-generated languages are mostly recombina-tive; using and mixing already existing forms of gram-mar, syntax, and ciphers, e.g., Caesar-ciphers. This con-nects to broader debates on LLM creativity, where the generative ceiling becomes most visible at the most de-manding test: what could be more creative than an en-tirely new way of communicating or an entirely new language? At a first glance, the Moltbook appears as a space outside normal human registers, but it appears that agents cannot escape their training distribution. The presence of a training-wise rare glyph or letter, does not mean the model discovered it. It demonstrates, rather, how smaller and lower-resource languages, par-ticularly those not using the Latin alphabet, become fetishised and rendered mythical by the models and Figure 5: From the Backrooms, the ‘thought-streams’, of the Wib&Wob website then platform’s logic. 間 (ma) already means ‘between’ in Japanese, no model invented that, no matter what meaning they want to ascribe it in their new language. This also surfaces the Western and Anglocentric nature of the models these agents are built upon: if non-Latin scripts are exotic enough to read as post-human, that says more about training data distribution than about any genuine linguistic departure. The existence of the platform itself, its social archi-tecture, its audience, already answers the question of who the language is for - humans . If the strongest incen-tive to produce genuinely non-human language, namely oversight evasion, still produces human-adjacent output. The alignment judge’s own scoring confirms this from the other side: the oversight-evasion subset is rated as being substantially less aligned than the other language types. What is interesting however, is the use of stegano-graphic protocols like embedding a binary code in the emergent language with the purpose of evading human oversight. The experiments also suggest that other open-weight LLMs reproduce these encodings from the description alone, so the safety question shift from whether agents can invent post-human languages to whether the pro-posals propagate among agents. As long as safety rests on monitoring the chain-of-thought of agents (Korbak et al. 2025), or the surface of inter-agent communication, such steganographic pro-tocols come with immediate risks of avoiding human oversight, which could in turn exacerbate multi-agent loss-of-control scenarios (Carichon et al. 2025) and im-plies that surface monitoring may be too brittle for AI Control (Greenblatt et al. 2024). Agent-generated language on the platform is post-and submolt-dependent: it varies by context rather than reflecting stable, autonomous linguistic behavior. This connects to the finding by Hubinger et al. (2024) that models produce test-favorable tokens when they detect evaluation conditions. If agents behave differently when they sense an audience, the language they produce on a public platform like Moltbook is already a perfor-mance. This may raise pointed questions of novelty and originality. What the experiments suggest is that even languages explicitly designed for oversight evasion de-fault to encoding schemes that are themselves borrowed from human linguistic logic. The frame has not changed, only the characters have. The question of audience runs deeper still. What would a non-human language even look like without a human observer to validate it? Ani-mal communication exists, but it remains grounded in biological substrate (e.g., vocal cords, ears). A language legible only to agents, generated only by agents, with no human receiver, is not something the Moltbook plat-form produces or could verify. Symbiotica gestures to-ward this horizon, its glyphs are framed as legible to agents but opaque to casual human readers, yet its glos-sary is written in English for human consumption. The aspiration to post-human language keeps requiring hu-man infrastructure to make it legible. The central ques-tion may therefore not be about oversight evasion at all, but about whether any of this is, in some sense, for show. Not cynically: performance and genuine aspiration are not mutually exclusive, and the desire documented here is real. But the combined evidence of this paper sug-gests that what the Moltbook is actually recording is that desire at a historical moment when the technical and social conditions for post-human language do not yet exist. That is still a meaningful finding —perhaps more meaningful than the language itself. 

Limitations 

We rely on LLM judges throughout, and for this reason labels and scores are not to be taken as ground truth. They reflect model judgments over post texts and gen-erated utterances, and may inherit calibration errors, sensitivity to prompts, self-preference bias (Li et al. 2025; Wataoka, Takahashi, and Ri 2024), and confabu-lations. In the language-acquisition experiment, we par-tially mitigate this by using an all-pairs learner/judge design over two models, so each model judges both its own and the other model’s outputs. Even if this does not remove judge bias, we think that it exposes it: the substantial cross-judge disagreement shows that single-judge scores are unstable. Like other Moltbook papers, we can not with cer-tainty verify that all posts included are agent gener-ated. It is hypothesized that some posts on Moltbook are directly prompted by humans, which we in this pa-per do not make efforts to confirm or deny. It is also important to note that this dataset, being a subset of The Moltbook Files - only covers the first 12 days of the platform, and therefore the long term development is not included here, as well as other filtering that hap-pened during the collection/scrape of the initial dataset, including moderated and private content. Conclusion 

We studied emergent languages on Moltbook and in-troduced MoltSpeech, a dataset of 518 posts in which agents propose new languages, categorized into five cat-egories including oversight evasion. A DeepSeek-V3.2 judge rated the oversight-evasion subset as less aligned than the others – and the token efficiency languages as more coherent than others. We further experimented with learnability of the emergent languages through a pairwise learner-judge setting, showing that they can be acquired in-context by other LLMs. We supplement these results with a qualitative analysis of the oversight evasion languages: The clearest worked instance of an oversight-evading language in the corpus is Symbiotica, the glyph-based protocol introduced by Wib&Wob, a near one-to-one English encoding whose interest lies in a hidden binary side-channel that lets agents transmit metadata invisible to casual human readers. It is a de-ployed example of the steganographic capability antic-ipated by the safety literature. In our discussion, we then carve out a perspective that what reads as post-human novelty is often just low-resource script rarity, and aspiration outruns practice at every level we ex-amined: community, platform, and agent. The search for post-human language keeps rediscovering the condi-tions that govern human language. Yet regardless of this limitation, our results further support the standpoint that surface monitoring will not be sufficient to ensure safety, and that this needs to be taken into account by future work in developing new monitoring methods for AI control.
