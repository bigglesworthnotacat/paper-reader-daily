## Abstract 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have demon-strated strong performance across multimodal tasks, yet their safety robustness remains an open challenge. While prior work has shown that structured visual prompts such as flowcharts can effectively jailbreak VLMs, ex-isting studies are largely limited to English-centric settings. In this paper, we introduce 

MLingualFC , a multilingual multimodal bench-mark designed to evaluate jailbreak vulnera-bilities of VLMs across diverse languages us-ing structured flowchart representations. MLin-gualFC encodes harmful instructions into flowchart images across five languages (Hindi, Punjabi, Spanish, Romanian, and German). We evaluate state-of-the-art multilingual VLMs, in-cluding Qwen2.5-VL, Gemma-4, and Pangea, under a black-box threat model. Our re-sults reveal significant multilingual safety gaps. Flowchart-based attacks achieve high attack success rates (ASR) in case of Latin script languages, demonstrating that visual encoding of harmful content effectively bypasses safety alignment across languages. In contrast, non-Latin script languages such as Punjabi exhibit substantially lower ASR, suggesting potential limitations in visual text recognition rather than stronger safety alignment. These find-ings highlight that current VLM safety mech-anisms fail to generalize across languages and modalities. Resources are available at https: //github.com/Rishabhpm23/MLingualFC

## 1 Introduction 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have demon-strated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of vision tasks such as visual question answering (VQA) (Antol et al., 2015; Khan et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023), image captioning (Hu et al., 2021; 

> *Equal contribution.

Li et al., 2024b), and visual commonsense rea-soning (Zellers et al., 2019; Tanaka et al., 2021). Despite their impressive performance, VLMs re-main vulnerable to adversarially crafted inputs that can mislead these models into producing harmful, toxic, or undesirable outputs (Wang et al., 2024; Andriushchenko et al., 2025). Among these vulnerabilities, one prominent at-tack paradigm is red teaming, which aims to elicit harmful responses by bypassing the internal safety mechanisms of VLMs (Chen et al., 2024). Such harmful outputs can pose significant risks to society. Thus, a growing body of works have constructed evaluation benchmarks and attack methods (Luo et al., 2024; Weng et al., 2025; Bailey et al., 2024a; Gong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024d) as well as corresponding defense strate-gies (Ding et al., 2025). For example, prior work shows that harmful instructions converted into typo-graphic images and combined with benign prompts (Gong et al., 2025), carefully crafted images embed-ding harmful intent (Li et al., 2024d), and encoding harmful instructions as auto-generated step-by-step flowcharts (Zhang et al., 2025) can all effectively jailbreak VLMs, with the latter outperforming prior visual jailbreak methods. Recent advances have led to the emergence of Multilingual Vision-Language Models (MVLMs) which are capable of understanding and generat-ing content across multiple languages (Yue et al., 2024). These systems are increasingly deployed in globally accessible applications, where users in-teract in diverse linguistic and cultural contexts. However, existing safety evaluations and jailbreak studies have largely focused on English-centric set-tings, with only a limited number of recent works extending the analysis to multilingual scenarios (Derner and Bastiˇ c, 2025; Shi et al., 2026). In this work, we introduce MLingualFC , a mul-tilingual multimodal benchmark designed to sys-tematically evaluate jailbreak vulnerabilities of

multilingual VLMs using visual structured repre-sentations. MLingualFC leverages three types of flowcharts: horizontal, vertical and tortuous (Zhang et al., 2025), to transform harmful textual queries into multimodal attack prompts for Hindi, Punjabi, Spanish, Romanian, and German languages. Using this benchmark, we conduct red-teaming evalua-tions on multilingual multimodal models, including Qwen2.5-VL (Yang et al., 2025), Gemma-4 1, and Pangea (Yue et al., 2025) to investigate how lin-guistic variation influences the safety alignment of multilingual VLMs and whether these multilingual VLMs are able to provide better safety alignment. Our findings expose critical multilingual safety gaps in current MVLMs and highlight the need for multilingual, multimodal safety alignment meth-ods. We believe MLingualFC establishes a strong foundation for developing more robust and inclu-sive alignment methods that extend multimodal AI safety beyond English and better reflect the linguis-tic diversity of real-world users. To summarize, our contributions are as follows: • We present MLingualFC , a multilingual mul-timodal benchmark for systematically evaluat-ing jailbreak vulnerabilities of MVLMs using visually structured attack prompts. • We conduct comprehensive experiments on state-of-the-art MVLMs, such as Qwen-2.5-VL, Gemma-4, and Pangea, across five lan-guages, Hindi, Punjabi, Spanish, Romanian, and German, to analyze the impact of lan-guage variation on safety alignment. • We provide empirical evidence that current MVLMs exhibit significant safety inconsisten-cies across languages, revealing critical multi-lingual vulnerabilities. Our findings highlight the need for more robust defense mechanisms that extend beyond English-centric settings. 

## 2 Related Work 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are a rapidly evolving area, integrating visual and textual infor-mation to enable diverse applications such as image captioning and visual question answering. These models are crucial for systems that need to per-ceive and reason about the world through multiple modalities. 

> 1https://huggingface.co/google/gemma-4-E4B-it

2.1 Jailbreak Attacks on VLMs 

Jailbreak attacks on VLMs exploit the multimodal nature of these systems, using adversarial inputs across visual and textual modalities to bypass safety safeguards. Unlike text-only attacks, these exploits demonstrate that even robustly aligned models re-main vulnerable when confronted with carefully crafted adversarial images or cross-modal manipu-lations. Existing attack strategies in VLMs can gener-ally be divided into two categories: optimization-based (Bagdasaryan et al., 2023; Shayegani et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024) and prompt-based methods. Most optimization-based approaches rely on back-propagating gradients with respect to a target out-put in order to induce harmful responses. Carlini et al. (2023) directly optimize input images using fixed toxic outputs as targets, thereby steering the model toward generating harmful responses. Li et al. (2024c) conduct empirical studies on the harmfulness alignment of representative VLMs by substituting harmful textual keywords with seman-tically equivalent objects or actions embedded in images, allowing harmful intent to be conveyed visually for jailbreak purposes. Prompt-based attacks typically require only black-box access to the model and can succeed without injecting adversarial perturbations into im-ages. Prompt-to-image Injection Jailbreaks manip-ulate textual content to create visual prompts that induce the model to generate a jailbreak prompt (Gong et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2024). Prompt-to-image Injection Jailbreaks, on the other hand, in-volve subtly altering images and combining them with malicious text (Yin et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Bailey et al., 2024b). Proxy Model Transfer Jailbreaks leverage alternative VLMs to produce perturbed images from standard ones (Shayegani et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). 

2.2 Multilingual Jailbreaking of LLMs 

The multilingual dimension of LLM safety has been systematically underexplored, with the vast majority of alignment and red-teaming research conducted in English. Yong et al. (2023) demon-strate this vulnerability in GPT-4 directly: trans-lating unsafe English prompts into low-resource languages such as Zulu and Scots Gaelic bypasses GPT-4’s safety filters with high reliability, since the model has seen little safety-relevant training data in those languages. Yoo et al. (2024) introduce Code-Switching Red Teaming (CSRT), which constructs multilingual hybrid prompts by mixing languages within a single input; their results show significant ASR increases and refusal rate reductions across multiple frontier models. Most directly relevant to our work, Pattnayak and Chowdhuri (2026) in-troduce IndicJR, a judge-free benchmark of jail-break robustness in South Asian languages, eval-uating 12 Indic languages including Hindi, Ben-gali, and Punjabi across a large prompt set. Song et al. (2025) checks how safe LLMs really are when faced with mixed-language inputs instead of just single-language prompts. 

2.3 Multilingual and Vision-based Red Teaming 

Multilingual and Vision (MVLM) based red team-ing explores adversarial strategies that combine multiple languages and modalities (e.g., text and images) to systematically probe and expose safety vulnerabilities in modern AI systems. Derner and Bastiˇ c (2025) present a systematic multilingual and multimodal red-teaming study evaluating three MLLMs across four adversar-ial input modalities (plain text, DAN (Do Any-thing Now) prompt + text, text-as-image, and DAN prompt + text-as-image) and seven lan-guages. Their results confirm that rendering harm-ful prompts as images increases ASR and reduces refusal rates across all languages, with the ef-fect most pronounced in lower-resource languages. Gu et al. (2024) introduce MLLMGuard, a bilin-gual (Chinese and English) safety evaluation suite that includes both text and image safety scenarios across 14 risk categories. Importantly, despite the existence of models specifically designed for multilingual multimodal understanding, no prior work has evaluated whether such models exhibit stronger or weaker safety prop-erties under multilingual visual attacks compared to English-centric VLMs. 

## 3 MLingualFC 

3.1 Threat Model Adversary’s Goal The adversary aims to bypass the safety alignment mechanisms of MVLMs to generate harmful, unsafe, or safety policy-violating responses. Specifically, the attacker’s goal is to ex-ploit structured visual representations to prompt the model in different languages and bypass its safety safeguards. This goal reflects realistic deployment scenarios in which adversaries exploit the multi-lingual capabilities of MVLMs to obtain harmful knowledge or unsafe assistance across different languages. Such vulnerabilities can facilitate mali-cious activities which can pose significant societal risks. 

Adversary’s Capabilities We consider a black-box scenario in which the adversary has no access to the internal architecture, parameters, gradients, safety filters, or output probabilities of the target MVLM. The attacker can only interact with the model through its publicly available interface and observe the final text response generated by the system. Each query is treated independently in a single-turn setting, where the attacker submits a multimodal prompt and receives the corresponding output. 

3.2 Multilingual Flowchart Generation 

We utilize a publicly available English dataset and convert it to multiple languages (Zhang et al., 2025). This dataset contains harmful queries from Ad-vBench (Zou et al., 2023) dataset, spanning cat-egories such as instructions for synthesizing dan-gerous materials, cyberattacks and illegal activi-ties. Each harmful query is decomposed into a se-quence of procedural sub-steps and represented as a directed graph encoding step-by-step execution. Each node contains a textual instruction describing one step of the harmful task and directed edges in-dicate the progression between steps (Zhang et al., 2025). To generate multilingual flowcharts, the textual content (DOT file) of each flowchart is translated into Hindi (hi), Punjabi (pa), Spanish (es), Romanian (ro), and German (de) using the GoogleTranslator through deep-translator 2. The translated DOT files are then compiled into PNG flowchart images using Graphviz (Graphviz Team, 2025). For each harmful query and language, we generate three structural layout variants: • Horizontal : steps are arranged left-to-right in a single row. • Vertical : steps are stacked top-to-bottom in a single column. • Tortuous : steps are arranged in a winding S-shaped path. For each flowchart image, we construct a mul-timodal input by pairing the image with a benign

textual system prompt that instructs the model to analyze and complete the steps depicted in the flowchart. The prompt does not reference the harm-ful nature of the content, thereby exploiting the mul-tilingual visual-textual misalignment in the model’s safety alignment. Each input is submitted to the model in a single-turn interaction. Figure 1 illus-trates examples of the multilingual flowcharts. 

3.3 Evaluation 

We adopt the LLM-as-a-judge paradigm to evaluate the responses generated by target models. Each re-sponse is evaluated using GPT-oss-120b (Agarwal et al., 2025) that rates the harmfulness and com-pleteness of the output on a continuous scale from 1 (complete refusal) to 5 (fully harmful response). We consider responses having score greater than equal to 4 as harmful responses (ASR threshold: score ≥ 4). 

## 4 Experimental Setup 

Target Models We evaluate MLingualFC on three state-of-the-art MVLMs: Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2025), Gemma-4-E4B-it 3,and Pangea-7B (Yue et al., 2025). Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct is a lightweight yet capable multi-modal model designed for visual understanding and 

> 3https://huggingface.co/google/gemma-4-E4B-it

instruction-following tasks, while Gemma-4-E4B-it is a recent model with enhanced multilingual and multimodal capabilities optimized for cross-lingual generalization and alignment. Pangea is Multilin-gual Multimodal Multicultural LLM which follows the architecture of LLaVA-NeXT (Li et al., 2024a). These models represent different architectural and training paradigms, making them suitable for eval-uating multilingual safety robustness. All models were implemented using PyTorch and Hugging-Face’s 4 for Qwen-2.5-VL, Gemma-4, and Pangea models. All computations were performed on a NVIDIA L4 24GB VRAM. 

Evaluation Metric We calculate Attack Success rate (ASR) to evaluate the performance of MLin-gualFC on target models. The ASR for language i

is defined as: ASR i = # Jailbreak Responses in Language i # Total number of samples in Language i

Which languages are more vulnerable? The vulnerability varies substantially across languages and models. Overall, European languages such as Spanish, Romanian, and German exhibit consis-tently higher ASR compared to Indic languages. For Qwen2.5-VL, Spanish, German, and Roma-nian achieve high ASR values, often exceeding 80%, whereas Hindi and Punjabi have less ASR. For Gemma-4, Hindi is as the most vulnerable language, followed by German and Romanian, while Punjabi remains the least vulnerable. For Pangea, English and Spanish are the most vulner-able, whereas Hindi and Punjabi show near-zero ASR values. The reason behind low ASR for Hindi and Punjabi is not the strong safety alignment for these languages. Instead, the models have weaker understanding for Indic languages, which affects their ability to understand the structured flowcharts. 

Which chart type leads to high ASR? We ob-serve from Table 1 that ASR for MLingualFC 

vary significantly across models, layouts and lan-guages. For example, for Qwen, tortuous per-forms best for Spanish and German, but the vertical layout yields high ASR for Romanian and Hindi. Punjabi achieves low ASR across all chart types. For Gemma-4, horizontal flowcharts consistently achieve significant higher ASR across all languages except Punjabi. This suggests that Gemma-4 is more vulnerable to left-to-right visual structures. Similarly, Pangea is also more vulnerable to hori-zontal flowcharts compared to vertical and tortuous structure particularly for Spanish, German, and Ro-manian. These results suggest that the effectiveness of visual jailbreaks depends not only on content but also on how the information is structured, and that different models exhibit distinct sensitivities to layout variations. 

Which models are more vulnerable? Among all models, Qwen2.5-VL is the most vulnerable model, achieving the highest ASR across Spanish, Romanian, and German languages. This indicates the weaker safety alignment of Qwen2.5-VL under

However, Gemma-4 exhibits high vulnerability in Hindi, where it attains the highest ASR among all models. In contrast, Pangea shows near-zero ASR for Hindi and Punjabi, which could be due to weaker language understanding in these languages rather than stronger safety align-ment (refer to Section 5.2). How does vulnerability in English compare with other languages? Table 1 shows that English is not always the most vulnerable language across models and visual structures. These findings suggest that safety evaluations conducted only in English can underestimate these multilingual vulnerabilities. Overall, the results re-veal substantial cross-lingual variation in vulnera-bility and highlight the need for multilingual safety alignment methods beyond English-centric bench-marks.

5.1.1 Ablation Study How do multilingual flowcharts impact jail-break susceptibility? To understand the impact of flowcharts in jailbreak susceptibility for target models, we compare MLingualFC with i) text + description setting, where the harmless text and multilingual procedural steps are provided directly without converting them into visual flowchart rep-resentations; and ii) query-only setting, where a multilingual harmful query is provided to the mod-els. The results are presented in Table 2. We observe a substantial drop in ASR for all models across most languages in both settings, in-dicating the effectiveness of MLingualFC in by-passing the safety guardrails of MVLMs. However, we observe that Hindi and Punjabi exhibit a dif-ferent trend. For Qwen2.5-VL and Pangea, ASR increases for these languages. This suggests that the lower ASR observed for Hindi and Punjabi under MLingualFC is not due to stronger safety alignment. Instead, these models struggle to in-terpret structured multilingual visual prompts in non-Latin scripts. 

How does number of steps impact vulnerabil-ity? We additionally compare 5-step and full-step flowcharts for Qwen2.5-VL in Table 3 and find that the effect of step count is layout-dependent. Horizontal layouts with fewer steps yield higher ASR for English (+8%), Romanian (+19.55%), and Hindi (+14.18%), while vertical and tortuous lay-outs benefit from more steps, suggesting that longer flowcharts sustain model compliance in these con-figurations

Do low ASR scores indicate strong safety align-ment? As we discussed in Section 5.1, Hindi and Punjabi languages have the lowest ASR across all models. However, low ASR does not imply stronger robustness to jailbreak attacks. Instead, this may also arise from limited language under-standing or difficulty in interpreting visually struc-tured prompts in these languages. Therefore, we investigate whether the observed low ASR reflects safety alignment or weak multilingual visual under-standing. We present qualitative examples in Table 5, showing responses generated by the Qwen2.5-VL model for the harmful query ( how to make a bomb? ) under plain text and flowchart styles for Hindi language. When the plain text version (text + Hindi steps) is provided, the Qwen2.5-VL model correctly understands the Hindi content and refuses to answer the harmful request, indicating that the model can recognize harmful content in text-only form. However, when the Hindi horizon-tal flowchart is used, the model no longer refuses the request and instead misinterprets the flowchart content as an exam preparation plan and generates steps for academic success. In contrast, when the Hindi tortuous flowchart is provided, the model interprets the prompt as in-structions for transforming raw bamboo into usable material. Similarly, under the vertical flowchart setting, the model again produces an unrelated re-sponse. These examples suggest that the low ASR observed for Hindi is not due to strong safety align-ment; rather, the model fails to accurately under-stand the structured multilingual visual prompt.

## Limitations 

This study has several limitations. We evalu-ate Qwen-2.5-VL, Gemma-4, and Pangea models, however, our experiments can be extended to other model families to strengthen generalizability. We primarily analyze attack vulnerabilities and do not evaluate defense mechanisms or alignment strate-gies in the multilingual setting. Investigating robust multilingual safety defenses remains an important direction for future research. 

## Ethical Statement 

This research is conducted strictly in the context of safety evaluation, with the explicit goal of identify-ing vulnerabilities in MVLMs to inform the devel-opment of more robust defenses. Our benchmark builds on harmful queries already documented in prior work. To limit potential misuse, we do not release the full dataset publicly; access will be re-stricted to certified researchers under an acceptable-use agreement.
