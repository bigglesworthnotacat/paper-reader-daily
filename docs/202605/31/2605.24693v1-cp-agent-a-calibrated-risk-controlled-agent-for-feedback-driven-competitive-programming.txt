## Abstract 

Large language models still struggle with contest-level program-ming, while many agentic remedies rely on massive inference-time sampling or expensive multi-stage post-training. We study when execution feedback reliably helps an LLM CP solver and which mechanisms govern the gains. We model feedback-driven solving as a calibrated stopped process and identify three quantities: false-admission risk, program-level evidence against bad programs, and the active-state success hazard. Under held-out trace calibration and selection from a pre-declared finite controller manifest, the result-ing structural certificate lower-bounds the clean success probability before false admission. We instantiate mechanisms targeting these quantities as Dual-Granularity Verification, Test Augmentation, and Experience-Driven Self-Evolving, yielding CP-Agent . Without updating any parameters, CP-Agent raises Pass@1 from 25.8% to 48.5% on LiveCodeBench Pro and improves Refine@5 by 11.0% on ICPC-Eval. Across three LLM backbones, CP-Agent lies on the cost–accuracy efficiency frontier, and ablations show that each component primarily affects its corresponding certificate quantity.

CP is judged by hidden correctness and asymp-totic feasibility under strict time and memory limits, so plausible implementations earn no credit when the algorithm or an edge case is wrong, and software-engineering virtues such as maintainability or modular design do not compensate. Recent work has explored two directions: (i) tackling competitive programming problems through massive code generation combined with heuristic-based inference and execution-based pruning, exem-plified by AlphaCode [ 29 ], AlphaCode2 [ 27 ], and o1-ioi [ 12 ]; (ii) enhancing the intrinsic reasoning ability of LLMs, including large reasoning models [ 12 , 16 , 30 , 38 ] that are often trained with rein-forcement learning. While these directions improve performance, they often come with substantial computational cost-large-scale sampling during inference, or expensive rollouts during training. CP offers rich, executable feedback: compilation errors, test fail-ures, and counterexamples directly expose concrete failure modes [ 42 ].

This makes agentic solving a natural fit. Two gaps, however, re-main for agentic CP. First, the literature disagrees on which mecha-nisms reliably help. MapCoder [ 22 ] reports that additional test-case generation is unhelpful while AlphaCodium [ 40 ] reports gains; MapCoder and Oi-Assistant [ 54 ] find retrieval of similar problems useful, while AlgoSimBench [ 28 ] finds it unstable. Second, recent strong agentic CP systems increasingly invest in multi-stage agen-tic post-training [ 46 ]. In contrast, for training-free LLM agents on CP, it remains unclear whether tool orchestration alone improves performance and which mechanisms drive its gains. These gaps converge to one essential question: which control-lable mechanisms turn execution feedback into measurable gains on contest-level CP? We answer it through a calibrated risk-control view of feedback-driven solving. We analyze feedback-driven CP solving as a calibrated, risk-controlled stopped process. Its state re-duces to three certificate quantities: the residual bad-candidate enve-lope 𝜌 𝑡 , the accumulated program-level evidence 𝐼 𝑡 , and the active-state success hazard ℎ𝑡 . Each is moved by exactly one abstract action class: RiskProbe on 𝜌 𝑡 , EvidenceAcquire on 𝐼 𝑡 , and ContextAcquire 

on ℎ𝑡 . The calibrated false-admission UCB ¯𝑞 𝑡 ≈ ¯𝜌 𝑡 exp (−I 𝑡 ) fol-lows as a derived quantity. This one-to-one reduction yields a unified design-and-selection framework: for any pre-declared fi-nite manifest of controllers, it supplies a post-selection-valid lower bound on clean-before-false success. Guided by this framework, we design CP-Agent with one controller per action class: Dual-Granularity Verification contracts 𝜌 𝑡 , Test Augmentation adds to 𝐼 𝑡 ,and Experience-Driven Self-Evolving raises ℎ𝑡 . A frozen admission gate and a refinement loop complete the agent. Extensive empirical results across three backbones on two contest-level benchmarks, together with theory-grounded mechanism ablations on the five-controller calibration class, jointly validate CP-Agent ’s effective-ness and its calibrated certificate. Our contributions are threefold: 

• A calibrated risk-control theory for feedback-driven CP. We prove an admission-risk theorem and a simultane-ously valid finite-manifest selection rule over five abstract action classes (Section 2). 

• A theory-induced agent. We instantiate this controller as CP-Agent , a tool-using framework with no parameter updates (Section 3). 1

• Trace-calibrated mechanism evidence. On LiveCodeBench Pro and ICPC-Eval across three backbones, CP-Agent raises Pass@1 from 25.8% to 48.5% and Refine@5 by +11 .0%; cali-bration attributes the gains to TA (evidence), HP (bad-plan survival), and Experience (success hazard) (Section 4). 

## 2 A Calibrated Risk-Controlled Theory of Feedback Control 

A feedback-driven CP agent repeatedly faces the same decision: admit the current candidate, refine it, or acquire more information. ## 3 Method 

Motivation & Overview. CP-Agent is the instantiation of the selected controller 𝜋 ★: each abstract action class maps to a con-crete tool (Table 1). All manifest entries—prompts, the observable admission gate, the HP and TA interfaces, the retrieval budget, the historical memory snapshot 𝑀 ★, the write mode, and the max-imum horizon—are frozen before benchmark evaluation, either pre-declared or audited on held-out traces 𝐷 cal via Theorem 2.6. 

## 3.1 Dual-Granularity Verification 

RiskProbe and EvidenceAcquire (𝑚 ) act at different granularities: the former screens a reasoning state before it is admitted, the latter gates a complete candidate before it is trusted. CP-Agent realizes them as a Hypothesis Prober for the logged upstream gate 𝐺 HP 

> 𝑡

and a Solution Validator for the program-level admission screen inside Γ𝑡 . Separating the two roles prevents the upstream screen from being double-counted as calibrated program-level evidence. 

Hypothesis Prober. During early-stage reasoning, the agent of-ten needs to verify local claims that are hard to establish purely in natural language—e.g., whether a recurrence holds on random small instances, whether a greedy rule fails on a constructed coun-terexample, or whether an invariant is preserved by a transition. 

CP-Agent exposes a Hypothesis Prober that compiles and runs short C++ snippets for this purpose. Under the frozen prompt and decoding policy the backbone itself decides when to call HP from its observable reasoning state—no hand-written trigger grid—and every call/pass/fail outcome is logged for calibration. A surviving HP call contracts the posterior odds of an upstream reasoning error (Proposition 2.7) and tightens the residual envelope ¯𝜌 probe ,𝜋 𝑡 enter-ing Eq. (9) ; HP thus moves 𝜌 𝑡 (and the aggregate ¯𝑞 𝑡 ) alone, not the program-level evidence channel. 

Solution Validator. Once the agent produces a complete candi-date implementation, the Solution Validator compiles and runs it against the local test suite: any public/local failure rejects the can-didate and returns counterexample-level feedback. By default SV is a deterministic hard screen and contributes no calibrated statistical evidence; only when the suite uses fresh sampled tests or an exter-nally calibrated set is an all-pass event additionally folded into I𝑡 

as the binary survival channel of Proposition 2.8. 

## 3.2 Test Augmentation 

The action class EvidenceAcquire (𝑚 ) makes the admission gate more informative against public-test-passing errors and exposes the evidence-acquisition intensity as a frozen controller entry. Public test suites Tbasic are fixed and shallow, so passing them is a weak cer-tificate: candidates routinely clear Tbasic yet fail hidden tests in cor-ner regimes. Test Augmentation (TA) instantiates EvidenceAcquire 

by appending consensus-filtered checks beyond Tbasic ; an all-pass on the augmented suite enters I𝑡 as the binary survival channel of Proposition 2.8. 

Augmentation pipeline. CP-Agent implements test augmenta-tion via three stages. Input Synthesis. Given the statement, the agent synthesizes a C++ input generator based on testlib.h 2 and command suites that sweep corner regimes such as min/max values, degenerate structures, adversarial patterns, and random sizes. Ora-cle Labeling. Ignoring time and memory limits often makes it eas-ier to write a correct brute-force solver than an efficient one [ 18 , 40 ]; 

CP-Agent therefore executes independently generated brute-force solvers on synthesized inputs to obtain candidate reference outputs. 

Consensus Filtering. Because brute-force programs can still be imperfect, CP-Agent runs multiple solvers in parallel and majority-votes their outputs. Candidates are evaluated on augmented tests  

> 2https://github.com/MikeMirzayanov/testlib Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Wang et al.

only after passing public tests, so augmentation serves as additional diagnostic evidence after the basic admission screen. Budget and retries. The frozen TA manifest caps synthesis at 32 candidate augmented inputs per problem. This is an upper bound rather than a fixed suite size: the generator does not keep sampling until a nonempty suite is obtained, and there is no separate retention cap after filtering. Instead, CP-Agent retains all valid inputs that pass synthesis, compilation/execution, and consensus labeling within the cap; generator synthesis/compilation/execution may be retried up to five times. Consequently, GenRate measures the fraction of problems for which this capped protocol yields a nonempty suite, while the candidate-level evidence calibration treats the resulting suite as one binary survival channel.

## 5 Related Work 

Competitive programming agents. Among tool-augmented LLM agents that ground reasoning on deterministic execution feed-back [ 8 , 11 , 13 , 15 , 33 , 49 , 53 ], work on CP benchmarks [ 7 , 19 , 24 ,50 , 56 ] mostly raises accuracy by scale: dedicated training or mas-sive sampling [ 12 , 16 , 27 , 29 , 30 ]. Inference-time pipelines such as MapCoder, AlphaCodium, and CodeSim avoid retraining by iterat-ing generation and verification, but are session-local and discard repair experience after each problem [ 22 , 23 , 28 , 40 , 54 ]. Reflexion-style self-refinement [ 42 ] keeps verbal memory across problems but offers no admission rule for when retrieved memory should enter the loop. CP-Agent adds neither training nor sampling: a cali-brated controller ties generation, verification, and a frozen memory snapshot to a single held-out audit, closing the gap to a stronger reasoner [30] on ICPC-Eval Refine@5. 

Testing, risk control, and frozen memory. Randomized testing and generated checks have long driven program repair and synthesis [ 6,9 , 18 , 43 ], but are usually consumed as raw signals. We treat SV and TA as calibrated evidence channels via binomial calibration, conformal-style risk control, and anytime-valid evidence [ 1, 10 ,20 , 48 ], so coverage and post-survival risk are read off held-out traces. Experience enters as a temporal frozen memory prior with PAC-Bayes/MDL complexity control [ 35 , 35 ]: tools and memory are fixed on 𝐷 mem hist before 𝐷 cal and 𝐷 test (Section 4.2) so the audit remains valid; memory may still grow in deployment. 

## 6 Conclusion 

A calibrated risk-controlled controller substantially improves contest-level programming without additional training. The theory derives certificate quantities and abstract action classes that are audited on held-out traces and frozen before evaluation; CP-Agent in-stantiates them as Dual-Granularity Verification, Test Augmenta-tion, and Experience-Driven Self-Evolving, matches DeepSeek-V3.2-Reasoner on ICPC-Eval Refine@5, and provides trace-calibrated mechanism evidence for each action class.

Why recovery is possible. Dual-Granularity Verification (DGV) gives CP-Agent two independent executable feedback channels: 

• Solution Validator (SV) and TA surface program-level failure signals. 

• Hypothesis Prober (HP) checks local claims, brute-force com-parisons, edge cases, and alternative failure hypotheses. When these channels disagree, CP-Agent arbitrates the evidence rather than blindly trusting a single noisy verifier.

## E Datasets 

ICPC-Eval [ 50 ]. is a competitive-programming benchmark de-signed to reflect real contest settings. It is built from problems drawn from 11 recent ICPC contests (collected via QOJ and VJudge): start-ing from 139 problems, the dataset is cleaned and finalized to 118 

problems. It filters out problems that rely on crucial non-textual figures, interactive problems, or problems without an official ref-erence solution. For tasks requiring special judging, ICPC-Eval retains them and additionally implements 12 custom special judges (e.g., for floating-point outputs or multiple valid answers). All prob-lem statements are standardized into LaTeX for consistent parsing and model input. The benchmark matches ICPC-style problem di-versity and difficulty, and provides annotations over eight major algorithmic areas (e.g., DP, mathematics, graph algorithms, and data structures), with many problems spanning multiple areas to capture the compositional and challenging nature of ICPC problems. 

LiveCodeBench Pro [ 56 ]. is a challenging benchmark for eval-uating large language models on competitive programming. Its central goal is to mitigate overestimation caused by training-data contamination by maintaining a continuously updated problem set. The benchmark curates problems from top-tier contests such as Codeforces, ICPC, and IOI. Problems are collected as soon as possible after they appear in real contests, further reducing the risk of data leakage. 

## F Metrics 

Refine@K [ 50 ]. is an execution-feedback-based evaluation met-ric designed to measure an LLM’s ability to iteratively improve a solution under a bounded turn budget. In ICPC-Eval, each problem is solved through up to 𝐾 response–refinement chances, and the metric asks whether the model can “pass the test within response and refinement chances” (i.e., succeed within 𝐾 turns). Concretely, the evaluation is multi-turn: in the first turn, the model receives the full problem statement; in subsequent turns, the model is addi-tionally provided with its previous response and the corresponding evaluation feedback. Thus, a model is counted as successful under Refine@K if any of its first 𝐾 attempts (conditioned on prior code + feedback) produces a correct program. Compared with Pass@K (in-dependent resampling), Refine@K better captures the behavior of reasoning/reflection-capable models because it explicitly evaluates whether a model can use feedback to correct mistakes rather than merely getting lucky with another sample. 

# Turn [50]. represents the average number of correct response turns, computed only over problems that are successfully solved within the refinement budget. Specifically, for each solved problem, we record the turn index at which the model first produces a correct solution, and then average this index across the solved subset. A smaller #T indicates the model typically fixes its solution within fewer turns, suggesting more efficient use of execution feedback, whereas a larger #T implies the model often needs more trial-and-error refinement rounds before arriving at a correct program. 

Pass@1. measures the single-try success rate of code generation: for each problem, the model produces exactly one candidate so-lution (one response/submission). If this first solution passes the evaluation (e.g., all test cases), the problem is counted as solved. Pass@1 is the fraction of problems solved on the first attempt:

## I Extended Discussion on Related Work 

LLM Agents. Large Language Models have been increasingly de-ployed as autonomous agents capable of interacting with external environments and solving multi-step tasks. In the domain of web navigation, researchers have developed agents to autonomously browse and interact with online interfaces [ 17 , 26 , 36 , 55 ]. In the field of software engineering, agents such as SWE-Agent have been proposed to handle repository-level maintenance and development tasks [ 14 , 32 ]. Furthermore, the application of agents has extended to high-level intellectual domains, including automated scientific discovery [ 2 , 31 , 47 ] and data science analysis, where agents assist in data visualization and interpretation [ 25 , 52 , 57 ]. These advance-ments collectively demonstrate the potential of LLM agents to generalize across diverse and complex workflows. In contrast to the extensive body of work on autonomous agents interacting with dynamic external environments, a more focused stream of research investigates Agentic Reasoning—the use of deter-ministic tools to enhance the model’s internal cognitive processes. Unlike autonomous agents that use tools as “effectors” to change the environment state, this paradigm employs tools—primarily code interpreters (e.g., Python) and calculators—as “cognitive prosthet-ics” to ground the reasoning process itself. Early works such as PAL [ 13 ] and PoT [ 8] demonstrated that decoupling reasoning from computation significantly reduces hallucinations in mathemati-cal and symbolic tasks. Advancing this trajectory, recent frame-works [ 11 , 15 , 41 , 49 ] establish a more sophisticated interleaved reasoning–action loop. By integrating code execution directly into the chain of thought, these methods utilize deterministic feedback for iterative step verification and error correction, enabling the model to navigate complex logical paths with higher reliability. Although fewer in number compared to general agent papers, these studies highlight a critical distinction: for challenging reasoning tasks, the value of a tool lies not in its complexity or connectivity, but in its ability to provide deterministic, verifiable feedback that constrains the model’s probabilistic output. Competitive Programming. Program synthesis has evolved sig-nificantly from early deductive [ 34 ] and inductive [ 44 ] synthesis to neural approaches like DeepCoder [ 3]. However, the release of challenging benchmarks [ 19 , 24 , 45 , 50 , 56 ] revealed a harsh reality: early models failed near-completely on competition-level problems. AlphaCode [ 29 ] marked a major milestone in this do-main by leveraging massive sampling for competitive programming problems, while AlphaCode2 [ 27 ] and OpenAI’s o1-ioi [ 12 ] later adopted reinforcement learning and heuristic inference strategies to achieve gold-medal performance. Most recently, OpenAI o3 [ 37 ]demonstrated that general-purpose reasoning models could reach Grandmaster status on Codeforces without domain-specific hand-crafting. Since training such reasoning models is computationally pro-hibitive, recent research has pivoted to inference-time enhance-ments using hand-crafting LLM workflows like MapCoder [ 22 ], AlphaCodium [ 40 ], and CODESIM [ 23 ]. These methods simulate human problem-solving through iterative “generate-and-verify” cy-cles. However, they mainly rely on fixed, stateless workflows, which suffer from rigidity: they lack the adaptivity to scale reasoning ef-fort within a problem, and crucially, they fail to transfer experience across problems, discarding valuable debugging insights after each session. This limitation highlights the need for more dynamic and long-term evolving mechanisms.
