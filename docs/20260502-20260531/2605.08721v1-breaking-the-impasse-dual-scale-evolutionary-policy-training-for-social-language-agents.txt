Abstract 

While Reinforcement Learning with Verifi-able Rewards (RLVR) has proven effective for closed-ended tasks, extending it to open-ended social language games via self-play re-veals a critical issue: evolution impasse . Due to the vast strategy space, language agents frequently converge to homogenized behav-iors, leading to deterministic match outcomes that eliminate the gradient signals necessary for policy evolution. To tackle this issue, we propose Dual-scale Evolutionary Policy Training (DEPT) for social language games. DEPT introduces a time-scaled evolutionary perception mechanism that detects impasse by quantifying dual-scale value baseline di-vergence alongside match entropy. Upon per-ceiving the collapse, it then activates asymmet-ric advantage reshaping to dynamically mod-ulate the optimization landscape for interven-tion. Thus, our method effectively restores gra-dient signals and enforces sustained strategic exploration. Extensive experiments on mul-tiple social language games demonstrate that DEPT outperforms strong baselines, avoiding policy degeneration and driving the continuous evolution of social language agents.

However, these tasks are inherently closed-ended problem-solving, characterized by deterministic correct answers with straightforward question an-swering. Under such dy-namic social contexts, LLMs not only engage in coherent logical reasoning but also perform long-horizon planning, infer opponents’ intentions in real-time, and process complex social signals such as negotiation (Bianchi et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2024), deception (Hagendorff, 2023; Xu et al., 2024), and bargaining (Xia et al., 2024; Abdulhai et al., 2025). with the RLVR paradigm without requiring expen-sive human annotations (Duan et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2024; Guertler et al., 2025). More impor-tantly, this property makes self-play an effective pathway for scaling LLM capabilities (Silver et al., 2018; Bansal et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a). Using a single LLM acting as both players of the game and continuously optimizing through zero-sum competition, self-play can over-come the data scarcity bottleneck in post-training, and meanwhile establish an intrinsic automatic cur-riculum through opponent co-evolution of social decision-making skills. Existing self-play RLVR methods (Liu et al., 2025a; Yuan et al., 2025) have achieved remark-able success in games with limited action space (e.g., Tic-Tac-Toe, Kuhn Poker), by designing role-specific advantage estimation. However, these methods struggle with open-ended social games involving free-form natural language interactions, which constitute vast action space that often re-quires verbal influence on opponents in order to succeed. Without effective guidance in such un-bounded strategy space, agents easily become trapped in suboptimal solutions. This renders the training process highly liable to evolution impasse ,that is, the stagnant situation a cognitive agent encounters in its decision-making (Laird, 2019; Sumers et al., 2023), which manifests as determinis-tic match outcome distributions for self-play RLVR methods. In this situation, the value baseline con-verges to the static expected return that causes the advantage function to approach zero, leading to the vanishing of policy gradient and locking the agent in a suboptimal state (as shown in Figure 1(a)(b)). To break the impasse, it is critical to develop an effective mechanism for evolutionary perception and intervention during self-play. To address the above issues, we propose a 

Dual-scale Evolutionary Policy Training (DEPT) 

method to dynamically perceive evolutionary states and adaptively modulate the optimization land-scape. Specifically, we introduce a time-scaled evolutionary perception mechanism that maintains parallel value baselines: a fast baseline sensitive to recent fluctuations and a slow baseline anchored to long-term trends. By measuring the divergence between these baselines alongside match outcome entropy, we derive a quantifiable metric for evo-lution impasse. To restore effective gradients, we propose asymmetric advantage reshaping . By an-choring on the stable slow baseline and adaptively fusing historical value bounds from the fast base-line, this mechanism constructs a contrastive advan-tage signal. It effectively penalizes dominant out-comes while amplifying rare trajectories, thereby enforcing exploration. Our approach effectively intervenes in evolution impasse and restores the gradient landscape, driving continuous strategic evolution. Experimental results across multiple social language games demonstrate that DEPT out-performs strong baselines. Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We propose DEPT for social language games, which is the first to dynamically perceive evolu-tion impasse and intervene in self-play RLVR. (2) Our method develops the time-scaled evolutionary perception and asymmetric advantage reshaping mechanism, which leverages dual value baselines to quantify evolutionary states and restore effec-tive gradient signals. (3) Extensive experiments demonstrate that DEPT outperforms existing SOTA methods, effectively preventing policy degenera-tion while driving continuous co-evolution.

4 Dual-scale Evolutionary Policy Training 

To overcome the evolutionary impasse, we propose Dual-scale Evolutionary Policy Training (DEPT), an algorithm designed to dynamically perceive evo-lutionary states and adaptively modulate optimiza-tion landscapes to enforce continuous evolution. As shown in Figure 3, our algorithm leverages a dual-timescale value baseline to quantify the sta-tionarity of the training process and dynamically injects intrinsic motivation signals into the advan-tage estimation to enforce continuous evolution.

5.1 Experimental Settings Tasks We evaluate our method on three adversar-ial open-ended language tasks involving multi-turn conversation and decision-making: (1) Don’t Say It : Players aim to trick their opponent into say-ing a secret word while avoiding saying their own. The first to speak the word loses; otherwise, it is a draw upon reaching the turn limit. (2) Negotiation :Players negotiate to exchange five resource types based on private valuations, aiming to maximize inventory value. The player with the higher value wins, with ties declared for equal payoffs or reach-ing turn limit. (3) Two Dollar : Players bargain to split a fixed fund based on hidden role constraints. The player with the higher payoff wins; equal pay-offs or reaching the turn limit result in a draw. All three games are adopted from TextArena (Guertler et al., 2025). For further details, please refer to Section B.2. 

Baselines We evaluate our method against sev-eral self-play RLVR methods in zero-sum games: (1) SPIRAL (Liu et al., 2025a): The first fully on-line multi-turn self-play RL system for LLMs. It introduces Role-conditioned Advantage Estimation to prevent thinking collapse in zero-sum games; (2) 

MARS (Yuan et al., 2025): It modifies the multi-turn GRPO algorithm by introducing a turn-level advantage estimator and role-specific advantage normalization to stabilize self-play RL training; (3) 

GRPO : An adaptation of multi-turn GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) for self-play settings, where group ad-vantages are calculated separately for each role to preserve effective role-specific learning signals; (4) 

SPAG (Cheng et al., 2024): An offline RL method that designs a discounted reward scheme for adver-sarial language games. Implementation details for all baselines are provided in Section B.1.

6 Related Work 

LLM-based Reinforcement Learning Reinforce-ment learning for LLMs has evolved from Re-inforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to RLVR (Ouyang et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2025; Team et al., 2025; Yue et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025b; Tan et al., 2025; Nie et al., 2026; Tan et al., 2026), leveraging objective ground truth to unlock deep potential in mathematical reasoning and code generation. However, existing RLVR research fo-cuses on closed-ended tasks with well-defined an-swers. Real-world open-ended social interactions— such as negotiation and deception—are fundamen-tally different (FAIR et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2026): they involve infinite action spaces, lack unique correct strategies, and require real-time reasoning about dynamic oppo-nent intentions (Abdulhai et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2025a). Our work addresses the challenge of applying RLVR to open-ended social interaction while sustaining continuous evolution in dynamic environments. 

LLM Self-play Training on Language Game 

As a classical paradigm, Self-play (Silver et al., 2018; Bansal et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024) addresses data scarcity while con-structing an automatic curriculum through oppo-nent co-evolution. For language games in LLMs, SPIRAL (Liu et al., 2025a) and MARS (Yuan et al., 2025) introduce RAE to handle role asymmetry, achieving strong performance in limited action spaces. However, in more complex open-ended lan-guage domains, these methods suffer from evolu-tionary impasse: LLMs converge to homogeneous behaviors, collapsing outcome distributions into de-terministic results. This drives advantages toward zero, causing gradient vanishing and trapping mod-els in suboptimal states. Our DEPT addresses this through dual-baseline perception to detect stagna-tion, combined with asymmetric advantage reshap-ing to restore optimization landscape and ensure continuous policy evolution. 

7 Conclusions 

In this paper, we address the critical challenge of evolution impasse in applying self-play RLVR to open-ended social language games, where agent training is susceptible to gradient vanishing and strategic stagnation. We propose DEPT, which dy-namically perceives evolutionary states and adap-tively adjusts the optimization landscape via dual-timescale value baselines. By quantifying stag-nation through baseline divergence and match en-tropy, DEPT selectively intervenes to penalize dom-inant outcomes while amplifying rare trajectories, effectively restoring gradient signals and enforcing continuous strategic exploration. Extensive em-pirical results confirm that our approach not only surpasses SOTA methods but also maintains supe-rior out-of-distribution generalization capabilities. 

Limitations 

While DEPT demonstrates substantial improve-ments in preventing evolutionary impasse dur-ing self-play training, several limitations offer av-enues for future research. (1) Self-play reinforce-ment learning inherently demands computational resources. Our experiments required approximately 30 GPU-hours per run on 8 NVIDIA A800 GPUs. These resource requirements may limit accessi-bility for researchers with constrained computa-tional budgets. (2) Following the reasoning-then-acting paradigm, our method generates extended re-sponse sequences containing both reasoning traces (ct) and executable actions ( at). This increases inference latency compared to direct action gen-eration, a common trade-off in test-time scaling approaches. The computational cost becomes more pronounced when scaling to longer interaction hori-zons or larger LLM backbones. We believe our dual-scale evolutionary policy training method pro-vides a principled foundation that could inspire novel mechanisms for detecting and intervening in more complex evolutionary dynamics, and we hope this work motivates the community to explore these promising directions.

E Computational Budget Analysis of Dual-baseline 

We analyze the computational overhead introduced by DEPT. Our approach shares the same core archi-tecture as existing methods, including multi-turn rollouts and policy updates. Since all compared approaches are critic-free and operate with a sin-gle actor LLM, they incur identical GPU mem-ory usage and LLM rollout costs. The primary addition of DEPT lies in the dual-baseline mech-anism for advantage estimation, as described in Section 4. To quantify the computational costs, we train an LLM agent on Don’t Say It using Qwen3-8B-Base and record a detailed breakdown of per-iteration training time. As illustrated in Figure 9, the dominant operations—rollouts and policy up-dates—account for 270.4s per iteration. In contrast, the additional components introduced by DEPT in-cur negligible overhead: advantage estimation via dual baselines requires only 0.0042s, constituting less than 0.0016% of the total per-iteration training time. This minimal computational burden stems from the fact that our dual-baseline advantage es-timation only involves lightweight arithmetic op-erations on scalar values, without requiring any forward or backward passes through the large-scale LLM parameters.
